## LinkedIn Post

When you map a protein-protein interaction network — thousands of proteins, tens of thousands of connections — the first thing you see are the hubs.

p53. HSP90. UBC. EGFR. Massively connected proteins sitting at the center of the graph, surrounded by radiating connections. The eye reads them as controllers. The CEOs. The central planners of the cell.

But the eye is wrong.

Remove a hub protein from a biological network and the network holds. It reroutes. It compensates. The remaining nodes pick up the function through distributed redundancy.

Now remove the central node from a star network — a top-down hierarchy — and the entire system collapses. Immediately.

We tested this. Real PPI data from STRING and BioGRID. E. coli. Yeast. Human. Every biological network we analyzed showed the same pattern: decentralized architecture that survives targeted attack.

The cell is not a corporation. It is a free market. The hubs are not kings — they are the most useful service providers in a voluntary exchange economy. They are connected because they are needed, not because they command.

Biology solved the central planning problem two billion years before Hayek wrote about it.
