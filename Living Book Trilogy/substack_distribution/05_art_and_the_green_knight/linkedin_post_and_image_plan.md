## LinkedIn Post

My grandfather was orphaned at nine.

Born Arthur Charles Farrington in Detroit, 1921. His father left. His mother couldn't cope. He was passed between relatives and foster homes until a man named Joseph Clokey — a composer, a church musician — adopted him and gave him his name.

Art Clokey.

He studied film under Slavko Vorkapich at USC. In 1953, he made a short film called Gumbasia — abstract clay shapes moving to jazz music. No characters. No plot. Just clay, alive, dancing.

From that experiment, Gumby was born. A green clay figure who could walk into any book, any story, any world — because his shape was not fixed. He was infinitely malleable, infinitely curious, infinitely free.

Art named his studio Prema — the Sanskrit word for universal love. He ran it for sixty years. He was offered fortunes to sell. He never did.

He didn't build Gumby to make money. He built Gumby to prove something: that the living thing always defeats the rigid thing. That imagination is sacred. That a formless piece of green clay, animated by love, can walk into any story and come out the other side unchanged.

That is the inheritance I carry. Not money. Not IP. A philosophy encoded in clay.
