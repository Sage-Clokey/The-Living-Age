# Hello, This Is Sage.

---

## A Letter Before the Book

Hello. This is Sage.

I want to start this way because I have learned, after years of writing manifestos and outlines and pitches and plans, that the most powerful thing you can do at the beginning of anything is tell the truth simply. So here it is, simply.

My name is Sage Clokey. I am the grandson of Art Clokey, who created Gumby. I am the son of Joe Clokey, who spent the last years of his life trying to bring Gumby back on principle and died in the fight. I am twenty-three years old, I am finishing a degree in biomolecular engineering and bioinformatics, and I am writing this book because I believe the world is about to change in a way that most people have not yet perceived -- and I believe I was put here to help explain why.

That is either the most arrogant sentence a young man can write, or the most honest one. I will let you decide by the end.

## What You Are About to Read

This book is called The Living Age, and it is the first volume of a trilogy. The trilogy is about one idea expressed in three dimensions: philosophy, biology, and action.

The idea is this: life is not a machine.

That sounds obvious. It sounds like something you would nod at and move past. But I need you to sit with it, because the entire modern world is built on the opposite assumption. Our institutions treat people like inputs. Our medicine treats the body like a device to be repaired. Our technology treats nature like raw material to be optimized. Our politics treats society like an engine to be managed by the right mechanic. And all of it -- every bit of it -- is built on the premise that living things are just complicated machines, and that if we get the engineering right, we can control them.

They are not machines. They are alive. And alive means something specific. It means self-organizing. It means growing from within according to an internal logic that no external authority wrote. It means that the farmer does not make the tree -- the farmer plants the seed, and the seed makes the tree, because the code for the tree was already inside.

The first job was gardener, not king. That is where we begin.

## Why I Am the One Writing This

I did not choose this. I want to be clear about that, because the world is full of people who chose their mission the way you choose a major -- by scanning options and picking the most appealing one. I did not scan options. The options scanned me.

My grandfather made Gumby out of clay in the 1950s and spent sixty years refusing to sell. He named his studio Prema, the Sanskrit word for universal love, and he encoded a philosophy into every frame of animation he produced: that imagination is sacred, that the living thing always defeats the rigid thing, that green clay can walk into any story because its shape is not fixed.

My father inherited that legacy and spent eight years trying to protect it. He would not sell. He would not compromise. He held the line until his body gave out, and he died at fifty-six, and within a few years the property was acquired by a corporation that replaced the clay with two-dimensional animation and the philosophy with nothing at all.

I carry both of them. The planter and the tender. The man who proved it could be done and the man who proved what it costs. I carry their victories and their losses, and I carry the question that their lives left unanswered: how do you build something that survives the builder?

That is the question this trilogy attempts to answer.

## The Shape of What Follows

Book One is the philosophy. The Sagent Creed. It is the worldview that holds everything else together -- the understanding of what life is, what power is, what stewardship means, and why the spiral always outlives the square. If you do not understand the why, the how will never make sense.

Book Two is the biology. The science that proves the philosophy is not metaphor but mechanism. Protein-protein interactions, genome architecture, decentralized cellular economies -- the living world organized according to the same principles the creed describes, not because I projected my philosophy onto nature, but because I found my philosophy already written there.

Book Three is the action. The Living Age itself -- the enterprise, the technology, the media, the thing we are actually building. How you take a philosophy grounded in biology and turn it into something that grows in the real world, outside the distribution channels of the powerful, beyond the reach of the sword.

Philosophy. Biology. Action. Why. What. How.

Three books. One seed.

## A Word About Voice

You will notice that this book does not sound like a textbook. It does not sound like a startup pitch. It does not sound like a self-help manual or an academic paper or a TED talk.

It sounds like me.

I write the way I think, which is in spirals. Ideas return. Themes recur. The same truth appears in Genesis and in cell biology and in Star Wars, and I am not going to pretend those are separate conversations when they are clearly the same conversation happening at different frequencies. If that bothers you, this may not be your book. If it excites you, welcome. You are hearing the music.

I am not an expert. I am not a guru. I am not a finished product. I am a twenty-three-year-old biomolecular engineering and bioinformatics student who grew up in the shadow of a legacy he did not ask for and a loss he did not choose, and who has spent the last several years trying to understand why he was given both. This book is what I found.

It is not the final word. It is the first seed.

And seeds, as you will learn in these pages, are patient. They do not need you to believe in them. They only need contact with the ground.

*So here I am. Sage. The grandson of the man who made the green clay walk. The son of the man who died holding the line. The third generation -- the one the spiral has been winding toward. I do not know how this ends. But I know how it begins. It begins with a word. It begins with hello. It begins here.*
