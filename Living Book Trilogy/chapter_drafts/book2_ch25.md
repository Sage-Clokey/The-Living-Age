# Nothing Is True, Everything Is Permitted

### The Creed Reinterpreted — Not Nihilism but Radical Freedom Under Natural Law

---

There is a creed that has been misunderstood for decades.

Four words that sound like nihilism — like the absence of meaning, the abolition of morality, the permission to do anything without consequence. Most people hear them and think: chaos. Anarchy. The void.

*Nothing is true. Everything is permitted.*

The Templars hear it and nod gravely and say: exactly. This is why humanity cannot be trusted with freedom. This is why we need control.

But that is not what the creed means. It never was. And the distance between what it sounds like and what it actually says is the distance between a civilization built on control and a civilization built on life.

The misreading is not accidental. The misreading is useful — useful to every institution that needs you to believe that without its oversight, there is only the void. If "nothing is true" means nihilism, then the institution is your only refuge from meaninglessness. If "everything is permitted" means license, then the institution is your only protection from chaos. The misreading turns the creed into the Templar's best recruitment tool: look what happens when people are free. Look what they believe. Nothing. And look what they do with that belief. Everything. You need us.

But the creed does not say what the Templars hear. It says the opposite. And the opposite changes everything.

## The Brotherhood Across Time

The Assassin Brotherhood, as the mythology of Assassin's Creed tells it, has existed across every era of human history. Ancient Egypt. Classical Greece. Viking Scandinavia. Renaissance Italy. Ottoman Constantinople. Colonial America. Revolutionary France. Every culture. Every tradition. Every era.

And in every one — the same creed. Not the same language. Not the same customs. Not the same weapons or tactics or gods. The same creed.

This is the first thing to understand. The Brotherhood is not an organization in the way empires are organizations. It has no central command. It has no doctrine of uniformity. It does not require its members to look the same, pray the same, fight the same, or think the same. It requires only alignment with a principle that is older than any single culture and deeper than any single tradition.

Altair Ibn-La'Ahad was a Syrian. A Muslim. A man formed by the traditions of the medieval Middle East — by the mathematics of the Islamic golden age, by the architecture of Damascus, by the call to prayer echoing off limestone walls. His creed was shaped by the specific contours of his world — by the Crusades, by the collision of civilizations, by the particular beauty and violence of twelfth-century Syria. And yet the principle he served was not Syrian. It was not Muslim. It was not medieval. It was the same principle that would surface, in different clothing, in every age that followed.

Ezio Auditore was an Italian. A Renaissance man. Formed by art and politics and the Church and the streets of Florence — by Leonardo's drawings and Machiavelli's calculations and the sound of bells across the Arno. He came to the creed through grief — through the murder of his family by the machinery of power. His path was personal before it was philosophical. He did not seek the Brotherhood because he agreed with its principles. He found the Brotherhood because his experience confirmed what its principles described: that centralized power destroys, and the only answer is the individual who refuses to be controlled.

Connor Kenway was half Mohawk, half British. He carried two worlds inside him, belonging fully to neither, serving something larger than both. His creed was formed in the forests of colonial America, in the space between two civilizations that each claimed to be the natural order. He saw what neither civilization could see about itself — that the freedom the colonists proclaimed and the order the Crown defended were both hollow when they depended on the subjugation of the people who were there first. Connor's creed was the creed of the margin, the creed of the one who sees both sides and trusts neither, because both sides have built their truth on someone else's land.

Bayek of Siwa was Egyptian. His creed was formed by the gods of the Nile, by love for his son, by grief that became purpose. His Brotherhood was born not from philosophy but from loss — the specific, irreplaceable loss of a child to the machinery of power. Bayek did not become an Assassin because he read the right books or passed the right tests. He became an Assassin because the institution killed his son, and the grief burned away every illusion he had ever held about the benevolence of centralized authority. His creed was written in blood before it was written in words.

Eivor was Norse. A shield-maiden shaped by the sagas, by Odin's shadow, by the cold of a world that demanded strength before it offered understanding. Her creed was not gentle. It was forged in a tradition that valued courage over comfort and honor over safety. And yet the principle was the same — the refusal to bend to any authority that had not earned the right to command, the insistence that the individual's path is their own to walk.

Every one of them different. Every one of them carrying the same fire.

This is not despite the differences. It is because of them. The creed does not demand that you become someone else. It demands that you become fully yourself — aligned with the living order written into your nature — and trust that the body knows what to do with what you offer.

## The Templars: The Honest Villains

The Templars are not evil in the way villains are usually evil. They do not want suffering. They do not want chaos. They do not cackle in dark rooms and plot the destruction of the innocent.

They want order.

They look at human beings — flawed, violent, self-destructive, easily manipulated — and they reach the same conclusion in every era: people cannot govern themselves. They need to be guided. They need structure. They need someone at the top who sees clearly enough to make the hard decisions that the masses cannot be trusted to make for themselves.

And they are not entirely wrong about the observation.

People do make destructive choices. Societies do collapse into violence. Freedom does produce chaos, at least in the short term. The block does offer something the spiral cannot always provide: the appearance of stability. The Templar looks at the world and sees a reasonable case for management, and this is what makes the Templars the most honest villains in all of fiction. Their diagnosis is partially correct. Their solution is entirely wrong.

Human beings are not destructive because they are free. They are destructive when they are cut off from the living order — when the creed written on their conscience is suppressed, when they are managed instead of cultivated, when they are treated as machines to be controlled rather than living things to be stewarded.

The Templar confuses the symptom with the cause. He sees the violence of the unmanaged and concludes that management is the cure. But the violence is not the disease. The violence is the response to the disease — the thrashing of a living thing whose natural order has been disrupted by the very management the Templar prescribes. A caged animal paces, bites, destroys itself — not because it is inherently destructive, but because the cage has severed it from the conditions under which its nature can express itself healthily. The Templar sees the pacing and builds a stronger cage. The Assassin sees the pacing and asks what would happen if you opened the door.

The Templar's solution — the Pieces of Eden, the systems of control, the overriding of free will — does not heal the wound. It deepens it. Because you cannot produce living order by taking away the living thing's ability to choose it. A garden forced to grow in straight lines is not a garden. It is a factory. And the plants in a factory are not living things. They are products.

## The Pieces of Eden: The Managed Dream

The Pieces of Eden are the most honest symbol in the whole mythology.

They are not crude weapons. They are instruments of consent management. The Apple of Eden does not kill. It controls perception. It makes people believe what the holder wants them to believe. It manufactures consensus. It does not force your body into compliance — it rewrites your mind so that compliance feels like choice.

This is the block's ultimate tool. Not the sword — the sword is too visible, too costly, too likely to produce resistance. The narrative. The controlled story. The managed reality that makes submission feel like freedom and the cage feel like home.

Whoever controls the Apple controls the dream. And whoever controls the dream controls everything — because a population that believes it is free does not rebel. A population that has been given a managed version of reality does not seek the real one. The Apple makes the jar invisible. The ants fight and love and build and die, and they never see the glass, because the glass has been woven into the fabric of their perception.

This is why the Apple is more terrifying than any weapon in the mythology. A sword can only kill the body. The Apple kills the capacity to question — which is the capacity to be free. A person who has been struck by a sword knows they have been struck. A person under the Apple's influence does not know they are being controlled. They believe they are choosing freely. They believe their managed reality is reality itself. And this is the deepest form of bondage — the bondage that has been made invisible to the one in chains.

Stories bypass the programming, slipping past the walls built to keep people blind. But they are also the very tools used to create the illusion in the first place. Those who control the stories control the world. The Apple is the story-weapon — the instrument that turns narrative from a living thing into a tool of control. It takes the most powerful force in human civilization — the story, the myth, the shared dream — and weaponizes it.

The Assassins do not want the Apple for themselves. They do not want to replace the Templar dream with their own dream. They want to destroy the Apple. Because they understand something the Templars never will: you cannot cultivate a living thing by controlling it. You can only create the conditions for it to flourish on its own terms.

## The Assassin's Role

The Assassin is not the gardener who tells each plant where to grow.

The Assassin is the one who keeps the bulldozer away from the garden.

They work in the dark. They serve the light. Not their light. Not their version of the right order. Not their managed dream to replace the Templar's managed dream. The light. The living order that was always already there — in every culture, in every tradition, in every conscience — waiting for the space to emerge.

This is the hardest part of the creed to understand, and it is the part that separates the Assassins from every revolutionary movement that has ever traded one tyranny for another. The Assassin does not want to rule. The Assassin does not want to replace the Templar's order with the Assassin's order. The Assassin wants no order imposed from above at all — because the living order does not need to be imposed. It needs to be allowed.

The difference is everything. The revolutionary who seizes the throne has not defeated the Templar. He has become the Templar. The reformer who takes the Apple and uses it for good has not destroyed the instrument of control. He has validated it. The only act that genuinely defeats the Templar principle is the act of renunciation — destroying the Apple, refusing the throne, dismantling the architecture of managed consent and trusting the living order to emerge in the space that remains.

This requires a faith that the Templar cannot fathom. It requires believing that human beings, left to their own conscience, will find the living order on their own — not perfectly, not without suffering, not without the chaos that freedom inevitably produces in the short term, but genuinely, organically, in the way that living things have always organized themselves when the bulldozer is removed from the garden.

The Assassin's blade is not a tool of offense. It is a surgical instrument. It removes the specific nodes of centralized control that prevent the living order from emerging. It does not build a new order. It creates the vacancy in which order can grow. This is why the Assassins are not conquerors. They have no empire. They hold no territory. They build no institutions. They move through history like a scalpel through a tumor — precise, purposeful, and then gone. They do not stay to govern what they have freed. They trust the freed to govern themselves.

## The Creed Decoded

*Nothing is true.*

This is not nihilism. This is the most rigorous epistemic position possible.

No worldly authority is absolute. No empire, no pope, no institution, no Apple holds ultimate truth. Every human system is provisional. Every throne is temporary. Every law written by men can be rewritten by men. Every narrative constructed by power serves power, and the moment you forget this — the moment you accept any human system as the final word on reality — you have handed the Apple to whoever built the system.

Nothing is true means: do not mistake the map for the territory. Do not mistake the institution for the principle it was meant to serve. Do not mistake the Church for God, the Constitution for liberty, the Jedi Order for the Force. The thing that is true is always deeper than the system that claims to represent it. And every system, eventually, becomes more interested in preserving itself than in serving the truth it was built to protect.

This is not a claim that truth does not exist. It is the opposite — it is a claim that truth is so real, so foundational, so much larger than any human construction, that no human construction can contain it. The map is not the territory. The menu is not the meal. The institution is not the principle. And the moment you confuse the container with the thing contained, you have begun worshipping the jar instead of drinking the water.

*Everything is permitted.*

This is not license. This is obligation.

If no worldly authority holds ultimate truth, then conscience governs. The higher law supersedes every earthly law. When the institution demands what the living order forbids — when the throne requires what the creed on your heart rejects — you answer to the living order. Not because you have permission to do whatever you want, but because you have the responsibility to do what is right regardless of what the institution says.

This removes every excuse of institutional obedience. You cannot say: I was following orders. You cannot say: the system required it. You cannot say: my superiors told me to. You answer to something higher than the system. And that something is not nothing. It is the law written on every heart before any institution existed to claim authority over it.

Peter said it before the Sanhedrin: "We must obey God rather than men." Tertullian said it in the face of Rome. John Wise said it in colonial New England. The Assassins have been saying it since ancient Egypt.

Nothing is true — no human authority is final. Everything is permitted — therefore you are bound by the law that was there before any human authority arose. The law on your conscience. The creed written into your nature. The living order.

The creed, properly decoded, is not the absence of morality. It is the deepest possible foundation for morality — one that cannot be corrupted by the institution, because it does not depend on the institution. It depends on the relationship between the individual conscience and the living order that wrote itself into every human heart. This is the morality that no Apple can overwrite, no Templar can suppress, and no managed dream can replace. Because it was there before the Apple was forged and will be there after the last Templar falls.

## One Body, Many Members

Paul looked at the early church and saw what the Templars could never build and the Assassins always carried.

A body.

Not an institution. Not a hierarchy. Not a senate of approved beliefs. A living organism — many members, one body, each part different, each part necessary, each part following the same living code written into it by its Creator.

"The eye cannot say to the hand, 'I don't need you.' And the head cannot say to the feet, 'I don't need you.' On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable."

The Templar looks at diversity and sees chaos to be managed. The body looks at diversity and sees design.

The foot is not a failed hand. The Norse Assassin is not a failed Italian Assassin. The Muslim tradition is not a failed Christian tradition. The Water Tribe is not a failed Fire Nation. They are different members of the same body — each carrying a piece of the living order that the others need, each expressing the same creed through the unique form of their own existence.

The creed does not demand uniformity. It demands fullness. Become fully yourself — not the self the institution tells you to be, not the self the Apple manufactures, not the self the Templar approves. The self that was written into you before any system existed. The self that carries a piece of the living order that no one else carries, in a form that no one else can offer.

Trust the body. It knows what to do with what you bring. The heart does not need to understand the lungs. The lungs do not need to approve of the liver. Each part serves the living order by being fully what it is, and the body organizes itself without a manager — because that is what living bodies do. The unity is not imposed from above. It emerges from below — from the shared code, the common creed, the living law that every member carries within themselves. The Templar cannot build this, because you cannot manufacture what must grow. The Assassin does not build it either. The Assassin simply protects the conditions under which it grows on its own.

## The Eternal War

The Templars will always exist.

Because the fear that drives them will always exist — the fear of human chaos, the fear of freedom, the fear of a living order that cannot be predicted or controlled. In every era, in every culture, there will be those who look at the messiness of free people and conclude that the answer is management. They will always offer order. Purpose. Direction. The Apple. The Piece of Eden. The managed dream.

And it will always look reasonable.

The Assassins will always exist too. Because the creed written on every human conscience cannot be fully suppressed. It re-emerges. In every era. In every culture. In people who have never heard of the Brotherhood but who feel — in their bones, in their conscience, in the part of them that knows — that no earthly authority has the final claim on their soul.

The war between the Assassins and the Templars is not a war that can be won. It is a tension that can be understood. The Templar's fear is real — humans are capable of terrible destruction. The Assassin's faith is also real — humans are capable of extraordinary self-organization when the Apple is removed from the equation. The war is not between good and evil. It is between two diagnoses of the same condition and two prescriptions for the same disease.

The Templar says: the disease is freedom. The cure is control.

The Assassin says: the disease is control. The cure is freedom.

And the living order — the body, the creed, the law written on every heart — waits beneath both prescriptions, patient, unkillable, re-emerging in every generation that finds the courage to listen to it.

## The Leap of Faith

There is a ritual in the Brotherhood. The leap of faith.

The Assassin climbs to the highest point available — a tower, a cliff, a cathedral spire — and jumps. No guarantee of safety. No certainty of survival. Only the trust that the fall will be caught. That the living order, which cannot be seen from the top of the tower, will be there at the bottom.

This is the creed enacted in the body. Not as philosophy. Not as argument. As a physical act of trust. You cannot reason your way to this. You cannot be argued into it. You can only stand at the edge and choose — to stay on the tower, safe and certain and controlled, or to fall into the arms of something you cannot see but know is there.

The tower is the institution. It is solid. It is high. It offers a view that makes you feel powerful and secure. From the top of the tower, the world looks manageable — ordered, mapped, controlled. The tower tells you: stay here. The ground is dangerous. The fall is death. Without me, there is only the void.

But the creed says: the tower is temporary. No tower is permanent. No height is the final height. No system built by human hands will hold forever. And the living order that the tower claims to replace was there before the first stone was laid and will be there after the last stone falls.

Everything is permitted. Therefore fall. Therefore trust. Therefore let go of the tower and discover that the living order was always waiting beneath you, ready to catch what the system said it could not hold.

We work in the dark to serve the light.

Not our light. Not our version of the right order. Not our managed dream.

The light.

The living order that was there before the first tower was built and will be there long after the last one falls.

We are many members. One body. And the creed was never given to us by an organization.

It was always already written on our hearts.
