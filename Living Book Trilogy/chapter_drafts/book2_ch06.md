# Chapter 6: Would Society Collapse Without Government?

---

## The Fear

This is the objection that always comes first. It comes before any other argument, before any other consideration, before any conversation about rights or consent or the nature of authority can get past its opening sentence. It arrives with the force of reflex — not because it has been carefully reasoned, but because it has been deeply conditioned.

Without government, who would build the roads? Without government, who would protect us? Without government, wouldn't everything just — fall apart?

The fear is real. I do not dismiss it. The person who asks this question is not stupid and is not arguing in bad faith. They are expressing what they have been taught since childhood: that human beings, left to their own devices, are incapable of coordinating. That without a central authority imposing order from above, the result is chaos. That the state is the thin membrane between civilization and anarchy. The fear lives in the body as much as the mind. It is the product of a lifetime of conditioning — every civics class, every news broadcast, every political speech, every disaster movie in which the government is the last line of defense against the void. The conditioning is so thorough that the fear feels like instinct, like something built into the species rather than something installed by the system that benefits from the fear's existence.

The fear is sincere.

It is also wrong.

---

## The Question Beneath the Question

Before answering whether society would collapse without government, it is worth asking what the question actually assumes.

It assumes that government is the source of social order. That the cooperation you observe around you every day — the courtesy, the commerce, the mutual aid, the intricate web of relationships and agreements and unspoken norms that allows millions of strangers to coexist — exists because the government created it or maintains it.

But is that true?

Do you cooperate with your neighbors because the law tells you to, or because cooperation is in your nature and in your interest? Do you refrain from stealing because you fear the police, or because you know it is wrong? Do you help a stranger change a tire because a government program incentivizes roadside assistance, or because something in you responds to another person in need? Do you love your children because the law requires it? Do you share meals with friends because a regulation mandates hospitality? Do you comfort the grieving because a bureaucracy filed the appropriate paperwork?

The overwhelming majority of the social order you experience every day is voluntary. It exists not because it was commanded but because human beings are social creatures who have been cooperating for two hundred thousand years — and whose ancestors were cooperating for hundreds of millions of years before that. Cooperation is not a behavior that was invented by government. It is a behavior that was refined by evolution over a span of time so vast that the entire history of human government is a footnote at the end of the last page.

The state did not invent cooperation. The state inserted itself into a pre-existing fabric of cooperation and claimed credit for the fabric. The jar did not create the ants. The jar trapped them — and then convinced them that without the jar, they could not survive.

## What the Living World Already Knows

Forests have no government. Brains have no central ruler. Ant colonies have no commander telling each ant what to do. Immune systems have no general issuing orders. Ecosystems have no administrator allocating resources. Markets — real markets, not the regulated simulacra that pass for markets in the modern economy — have no central planner.

And yet they work.

Not marginally. Not barely. They work with a sophistication and resilience that makes the most advanced human engineering look primitive by comparison.

The forest coordinates the distribution of water and nutrients across thousands of species over thousands of square miles without a single directive being issued from anywhere. The brain coordinates a hundred billion neurons in a seamless symphony of consciousness without a CEO neuron at the top. The immune system identifies and eliminates threats it has never encountered before, remembers them for decades, and does all of this without a central command structure. The coral reef — one of the most complex ecosystems on earth — maintains itself through the voluntary symbiosis of millions of organisms, each one pursuing its own survival, each one contributing to a collective order that no individual organism designed or comprehends.

The difference between living things and machines is that living things just need to be allowed to grow, and machines require constant effort to keep working. If you treat either as the other, it breaks. Treat a living thing like a machine — try to program it, control its inputs, standardize its outputs — and you kill the very quality that makes it alive. Treat a machine like a living thing — leave it alone and expect it to adapt — and it rusts. The Block treats society like a machine. The Spiral recognizes that society is alive.

These systems are not random. They are not chaotic. They are ordered — but their order is emergent, not imposed. It arises from the bottom up, from the interactions of individual agents following local rules, reading their immediate context, and responding with their own intelligence. No individual agent has a map of the whole. No individual agent needs one. The intelligence of the system is distributed across every node, and the collective behavior is more sophisticated than anything a central planner could design.

This is not an analogy. This is how the world actually works. And the question — when someone says society would collapse without rulers — is: what exactly are they assuming about people? Are they assuming that human beings are less capable of self-organization than ants? Less capable of cooperation than trees? Less capable of emergent order than neurons?

## The Historical Record

Governments have collapsed before. What happened?

When the Western Roman Empire fell, Europe did not descend into formless chaos. Communities reorganized. Local governance emerged. Monasteries preserved knowledge. Trade networks adapted. People did what people always do when the central authority that claimed credit for their cooperation disappears — they kept cooperating. The cooperation was never the government's. It was the people's. The government was the parasite that had attached itself to the host and convinced the host that the parasite was the heart.

The period we call the "Dark Ages" was, in many regions, darker mainly for the institutions that had depended on centralized power. For the people on the ground — the farmers, the craftsmen, the local communities — life continued. In many cases it improved, because the extractive apparatus of the empire was no longer siphoning resources upward to fund wars and palaces and a bureaucracy that served its own perpetuation. The tax collectors stopped coming. The conscription officers stopped coming. The people who had been feeding the empire were suddenly free to feed themselves, and many of them found that they could do it quite well without the assistance of the institution that had been taking a third of everything they produced.

Iceland operated for over three hundred years with a decentralized legal system — the Althing and the system of godar — that had no executive branch, no standing army, no police force, and no centralized authority to enforce its decisions. Disputes were resolved through negotiation, arbitration, and social pressure. It was not a utopia. But it was a functioning society, and it functioned for longer than many empires. It functioned precisely because the order was not imposed from above. It was grown from within — from the fabric of relationships, reputations, and voluntary commitments that constituted the actual society underneath the legal framework.

The frontier communities of early America governed themselves for decades before any centralized authority reached them. They built roads. They established schools. They organized defense. They resolved disputes. They did all of this voluntarily — not because there was no structure, but because the structure was local, consensual, and directly accountable to the people it served. The teacher was hired by the parents. The road was built by the people who used it. The disputes were settled by people who knew both parties and had to live with the outcome. The accountability was total and immediate — which is precisely what centralized government eliminates by inserting distance between the decision-maker and the consequence.

In every case, the collapse of central authority revealed not the absence of order but the resilience of order that existed independently of the state. The government did not create the cooperation. It merely sat on top of it. And when the government was removed, the cooperation remained.

## The Things We Already Do

Look at your own life. List the things that matter most to you — the relationships, the communities, the sources of meaning and support that actually sustain you on a daily basis.

How many of them were provided by the government?

Your family. Your friendships. Your faith community. Your neighborhood. The person who watches your kids when you have an emergency. The friend who drives you to the airport. The colleague who mentors you without being asked. The local business that sponsors the youth league. The volunteer fire department. The community garden. The mutual aid network that sprang up overnight when the pandemic hit.

None of these were mandated. None were compelled. None required a bureaucracy to administer them. They exist because human beings are wired for cooperation — because cooperation is not a behavior imposed from above but a capacity built into the architecture of the species, refined over millions of years of evolution, encoded in our neurology and our hormones and our deepest instincts. The oxytocin that floods your system when you hold a child. The mirror neurons that fire when you witness another person's pain. The deep, pre-rational impulse to help, to share, to protect, to belong. These are not products of civilization. They are the raw material of civilization. They are what civilization is built from — and they existed long before any government claimed to be their source.

The state claims credit for social order the way a rooster claims credit for the sunrise. The sun was coming up regardless. The cooperation was happening regardless. The question is not whether people would cooperate without government. The evidence of your own life answers that question every day. The question is what would happen if the coercive apparatus that extracts a third of everyone's productivity, that regulates every transaction, that claims final authority over every dispute — what would happen if that apparatus were no longer there?

## The Fear Is the Product

The fear that society would collapse without government is not a natural observation. It is a manufactured product. It is the single most important product the state manufactures, because it is the product on which all other state products depend.

If people believed they could govern themselves — if they truly, deeply believed it — the state would be unnecessary. And an institution that is unnecessary cannot justify its extraction. Cannot justify its wars. Cannot justify its prisons. Cannot justify the claim that it, and it alone, has the right to use violence.

So the state produces fear. It produces the belief that without it, everything falls apart. And it produces this belief not primarily through propaganda — though there is plenty of propaganda — but through structure. Through designing systems that make people dependent. Through replacing local governance with central governance so that when someone asks "who would handle this without the state?" the honest answer is "nobody, anymore, because the state destroyed the local institutions that used to handle it."

This is the mechanism that makes the fear self-reinforcing. The state dismantles the local institutions. The people lose the capacity for local self-governance. The people become dependent on the state. The state points to the dependency as proof that it is necessary. The circle closes. The jar convinces the ants that without the jar, there is only the void. But the jar is not what keeps them alive. It is what destroyed the world they used to live in and replaced it with a world in which the jar is the only option.

The state does not protect you from chaos. The state creates the conditions in which its protection appears necessary — by dismantling the alternatives, by criminalizing self-reliance, by regulating voluntary cooperation into illegality, and by educating every child from the age of five to believe that without a central authority, nothing works.

The jar convinces the ants that without the jar, there is only the void. But the jar is not what keeps them alive. The jar is what traps them. And the ants have been inside long enough to forget what they are capable of.

## What Would Actually Happen

Society would not collapse. Society would reorganize.

The same way an ecosystem reorganizes after a fire — not into chaos, but into a new and often more diverse order. The same way a market reorganizes after a monopoly is broken — not into dysfunction, but into competition and innovation. The same way a community reorganizes after a disaster — not into every man for himself, but into the most intense and beautiful cooperation human beings are capable of. Disaster research consistently shows this: when the formal structures fail, people do not descend into savagery. They ascend into solidarity. They share food. They shelter strangers. They organize rescue operations with no one in command. The state tells you that without it, people become wolves. The evidence tells you that without it, people become neighbors.

The schools would be replaced by schools that serve families instead of bureaucracies. The roads would be maintained by the people and businesses that use them — as roads were maintained for thousands of years before any government claimed a monopoly on pavement. Disputes would be resolved through arbitration, mediation, and the social pressure of communities where reputation matters — as disputes were resolved for the vast majority of human history.

Defense would be local, distributed, and voluntary — which, incidentally, is how the most effective defense in history has always worked. Centralized armies are good at projecting imperial power. They are terrible at defending communities. The Swiss, who maintained local militias for centuries and were never successfully invaded, understood this. The early American colonists, who distrusted standing armies as tools of tyranny, understood this.

None of this would be perfect. Voluntary society is not utopia. It is life — with all its friction and failure and complexity. But it is life that is alive. Life that adapts. Life that learns. Life that grows from the bottom up, responding to the actual needs of actual people, rather than from the top down, serving the perpetuation of an institution that lost sight of its purpose generations ago.

## The Deeper Truth

If we understand nature's code, we could design things in nature's way, not in the way of empire. Society is a living system, and it should act as one.

Every part in a living system has an individual purpose and no part of the living system is above another. They are all equal but perform different functions. The heart is not above the lung. The root is not above the leaf. The neuron is not above the blood cell. Each serves the whole by being fully itself — not by being commanded, but by being free. The body does not function because the brain controls every cell. The body functions because every cell carries the full genetic code, reads its local environment, and responds with its own intelligence. The brain coordinates — it does not command. And the difference between coordination and command is the difference between the Spiral and the Block.

People are like cells in a body. Each one carries the full code. Each one reads its local environment. Each one responds with its own intelligence. And the body — the society, the civilization, the living order — emerges from the interaction of all those individual intelligences in a way that no central planner could replicate and no central authority could improve.

Governed by the law of voluntary harmony — where genius is not permissioned, and every spark has the right to shine. This is not a dream. This is the description of every living system that has ever worked — from a single cell to a forest to a civilization that has not yet forgotten what it is.

---

*Forests have no government. Brains have no central ruler. The most complex and resilient systems on earth coordinate without command. When someone tells you that society would collapse without rulers, ask them what they are assuming about people. Because the assumption — that human beings cannot cooperate without coercion — is not a fact. It is a fear. And it is the most profitable fear in the history of power.*

*The jar is not what keeps the ants alive. The jar is what keeps the ants from discovering what they could build if they were free.*
