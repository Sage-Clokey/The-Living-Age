# Appendix B: Parallel Cases

---

> *The Living Age Trilogy — Appendices*

---

The Clokey story is not an isolated event. It is one instance of a pattern that has repeated across the children's entertainment industry for decades. In each case, the sequence is the same: an independent creator builds something original, the creation becomes culturally valuable, and a larger entity — a corporation, a financier, a media conglomerate — acquires or displaces the creator through financial leverage, contractual manipulation, or the sheer weight of institutional power.

The pattern is not coincidence. It is architecture.

What follows are four parallel cases, each documented from public records, court filings, published interviews, and industry reporting. They are presented without embellishment. The facts speak clearly enough.

---

## Case 1: Will Vinton — Claymation

Will Vinton invented Claymation.

Not figuratively. Literally. He developed the technique, named it, and trademarked the word. His Portland-based studio, Will Vinton Productions, created *The California Raisins*, the *M&M's* characters, *The PJs*, and the landmark television special *A Claymation Christmas Celebration*, which won the Emmy for Outstanding Animated Program in 1987. Vinton was nominated for an Academy Award for his short film *Closed Mondays* (1974) — the first clay-animated film to win the Oscar for Best Animated Short.

Vinton was to clay animation what Art Clokey was to Gumby: the originator, the craftsman, the person who saw what the medium could become before anyone else thought to look. He built his studio from nothing in Portland, Oregon — far from Hollywood, far from the networks, deliberately independent. He trained a generation of animators. He proved that clay, shaped by human hands, frame by frame, could hold an audience in a way that no corporate committee could replicate, because the life in the work came from the life in the maker.

In the late 1990s, Vinton needed capital. Stop-motion animation is expensive — labor-intensive, time-consuming, requiring skilled artisans who cannot be replaced by software. The economics of independence are always precarious, because the system is designed to make independence unprofitable. Vinton took an investment from Phil Knight, the co-founder and chairman of Nike. Five million dollars. It was a reasonable decision — the kind of deal that independent creators make when they believe a financial partner will respect the work.

Knight did not respect the work. Knight respected the asset.

The investment came with board seats. By 2002, Knight's representatives on the board — executives from Nike's orbit, people whose expertise was in athletic apparel, not animation — outnumbered Vinton's allies. They voted to remove Will Vinton as president of the company he had founded. He was fired from his own studio. Not for incompetence. Not for creative failure. For the same reason every independent creator is eventually displaced: because the institution does not need the creator once it controls the creation.

Vinton sued for corporate nepotism. The case was dismissed. The court saw a board exercising its fiduciary authority. The court could not see what the board had actually done: amputated the living thing from the person who made it live.

The company was rebranded as Laika Entertainment. Knight's son, Travis Knight, became president and eventually CEO. Laika went on to produce critically acclaimed stop-motion films — *Coraline*, *ParaNorman*, *Kubo and the Two Strings* — using the studio infrastructure, talent pipeline, and institutional knowledge that Will Vinton had built. The films are beautiful. They are technically masterful. But they are not Claymation. They are 3D-printed puppets augmented with CGI. The clay — the actual material, the medium that carries the artist's fingerprints — was thrown out. The thing that made Vinton's work alive was replaced with something more controllable, more efficient, more scalable. More square.

Will Vinton's name was removed from the company. His trademark on "Claymation" was transferred. He spent the remaining years of his life working on independent projects, largely outside the studio system he had pioneered. He held no grudge publicly, because that is the kind of person who makes clay come alive — they care more about the work than about the credit.

Will Vinton died on October 4, 2018, at the age of seventy.

Joe Clokey died on March 2, 2018, at the age of fifty-six.

Two independent clay animation pioneers. Both pushed out. Both died in the same year. The medium they championed — clay shaped by human hands, carrying the literal fingerprints of the artist — was declared obsolete by an industry that had never understood what the fingerprints meant.

## Case 2: Phil Vischer — VeggieTales

Phil Vischer created VeggieTales in 1993.

Operating out of his company Big Idea Entertainment in a spare bedroom in suburban Chicago, Vischer and his collaborator Mike Nawrocki produced a series of computer-animated home videos featuring Bob the Tomato and Larry the Cucumber — characters that taught biblical values to children through humor and storytelling. The concept was simple, the execution was warm, and the spirit behind it was genuine. Vischer was a man who loved children and loved scripture, and he made something that carried both loves without irony, without condescension, without the hollow performativeness that institutional religion so often produces.

VeggieTales became the bestselling direct-to-video series in America, moving over 75 million units. The franchise generated hundreds of millions in revenue. It proved something the industry did not want proven: that an independent creator, working outside the studio system, operating on faith rather than market research, could reach more families than the entire apparatus of corporate children's entertainment.

Big Idea grew rapidly — too rapidly, as Vischer would later acknowledge with the honesty that characterized everything he did. The company invested heavily in a theatrical film, *Jonah: A VeggieTales Movie* (2002), which performed modestly at the box office. A lawsuit from a former distributor, HiT Entertainment, resulted in a $14 million judgment against Big Idea. The company could not absorb the loss. Big Idea filed for bankruptcy in 2003.

At the bankruptcy auction, VeggieTales was purchased by Classic Media.

Classic Media — the same company that held the Gumby licensing rights. The same company that Joe Clokey spent six years partnered with in a joint venture that produced nothing — no movie, no series, no content of any kind. Six years of plans and announcements and silence, and then the license expired and the rights reverted to the Clokeys. Classic Media touched both properties. It produced nothing with either one. It functioned not as a producer but as a *holding pattern* — a place where independent IP went to sit quietly and accumulate value while its creators aged and weakened.

Classic Media acquired VeggieTales for $19.3 million — a fraction of the franchise's actual value, the way a pawnshop pays a fraction of value because it knows the seller has no other options. Phil Vischer retained no ownership. He later described the experience as watching his life's work "being carried away in boxes." The man who had built something alive — who had poured his faith, his humor, his love for children into characters that millions of families recognized and trusted — stood empty-handed while a corporation he had never heard of carried his creation through the door.

Vischer continued to create — he founded Jellyfish Labs and produced the series *What's in the Bible?* — because creators create, even when the thing they created has been taken from them. But VeggieTales was no longer his. The characters he made, the stories he wrote, the values he embedded in the work — all belonged to the company that bought them at auction, the way a plantation owns the field a farmer cleared.

Classic Media was later acquired by DreamWorks Animation, which was later acquired by NBCUniversal, which is owned by Comcast. VeggieTales — a series made by a man in his spare bedroom to teach children about the Bible — now sits inside a media conglomerate with a market capitalization exceeding $150 billion. Bob the Tomato reports, through seven layers of corporate hierarchy, to a board of directors whose primary obligation is shareholder return.

Phil Vischer created something alive. The machine consumed it. The tomato is still red. But the life that made it matter is somewhere else now.

## Case 3: Klasky Csupo — Rugrats

Arlene Klasky and Gabor Csupo founded Klasky Csupo, Inc. in 1982, operating initially out of their Hollywood apartment. Their first major work was animating *The Simpsons* during its initial run on *The Tracey Ullman Show* and its early seasons as a standalone series — the animation that established the visual identity of what would become the longest-running American animated series in television history. But the Simpsons belonged to Matt Groening and Fox. Klasky and Csupo were the hands, not the owners.

In 1991, they created something that was theirs: *Rugrats*, commissioned by Nickelodeon.

Rugrats became one of the most successful animated series in television history. It ran for nine seasons, produced three theatrical films (the first of which grossed over $100 million domestically, making it at the time the highest-grossing non-Disney animated film ever), and generated an estimated $3.7 billion in merchandise revenue. The show won four Daytime Emmy Awards. At its peak, Rugrats was Nickelodeon's highest-rated program and one of the most-watched children's shows in the world.

Three point seven billion dollars in merchandise. Say that number slowly. Let it register. That is the retail value of the toys, the clothes, the backpacks, the lunchboxes, the bedsheets, the party supplies, the video games, the books — the entire commercial ecosystem that grew from characters that two people invented in their apartment. $3.7 billion.

And Klasky and Csupo believed they were not receiving their contractual share.

They did what any honest person would do. They hired independent auditors to examine the financial accounting on Rugrats merchandise revenue from Paramount, which was owned by Viacom, which was controlled by Sumner Redstone. They asked to see the books. They asked the simplest question in commerce: where is the money?

The audit was never completed. Nickelodeon terminated its partnership with Klasky Csupo. The studio lost its primary source of production revenue. Klasky Csupo went dormant for four years — the years that should have been their peak, the years when their creation was generating billions, the years when any just accounting would have made them wealthy enough to fund their own projects for the rest of their lives.

When you ask a question that reveals the answer the institution does not want revealed, the institution does not answer the question. The institution eliminates the questioner. This is not a business strategy. This is the behavior of a system that cannot survive transparency — a system whose power depends on the asymmetry of information between the creator and the controller.

Nickelodeon retained the Rugrats intellectual property. The network has since produced Rugrats reboots, specials, and merchandise — without Klasky or Csupo's creative involvement. The characters still exist. The merchandise still sells. The revenue still flows. It flows to Paramount Global, which is the current name of the entity that Sumner Redstone built, which is the entity that terminated the partnership with the people who created the thing that generates the revenue.

Arlene Klasky and Gabor Csupo created characters that generated billions. When they asked where the money went, they lost the characters. That is the architecture. That is how the block functions. The block does not create. The block stands between the creator and the audience and extracts what passes through the gate. And when the creator asks to see the ledger, the block closes the gate.

## Case 4: Jim Henson — The Muppets

Jim Henson created the Muppets in 1955 — the same year Art Clokey created Gumby.

Think about that. The same year. Two men, working independently, on opposite coasts, both making characters by hand — one from clay, one from fabric — both building something that would define American children's culture for the next half century. Both independent. Both principled. Both driven by a love for the work that no corporate incentive could explain, because the incentive was not financial. The incentive was the thing itself.

Over the next thirty-five years, Henson built one of the most beloved and recognizable entertainment brands in the world. *The Muppet Show*, *Sesame Street*, *Fraggle Rock*, *The Dark Crystal*, *Labyrinth* — Henson's creations were original, handmade, and infused with a warmth and humanity that no corporate committee could have produced. The Muppets were not characters in the way that corporate IP produces characters — designed by committee, tested by focus group, optimized for demographic appeal. The Muppets were *personalities* — eccentric, contradictory, flawed, lovable in the way that real people are lovable. They were alive because their creator was alive in them, because Henson's own gentleness and humor and sadness and wonder were woven into every stitch.

In 1989, Henson entered negotiations with The Walt Disney Company to sell his company. The deal was complex and incomplete. According to multiple accounts, Henson was deeply conflicted about the sale — he wanted his characters to have a secure future, but he did not want to lose creative control. He was tired. He had been running the operation for thirty-four years. He wanted to create, not manage. He wanted someone to handle the business so he could focus on the art.

This is the creator's dilemma, and it is the same dilemma Art Clokey faced, and Phil Vischer faced, and Will Vinton faced. The independent creator cannot scale without help. The help that is available comes with conditions. The conditions always involve some transfer of control. And the transfer of control, once begun, does not reverse. It proceeds in one direction — from the creator to the institution — because the institution has lawyers and the creator has art, and lawyers are better at taking than artists are at holding.

Jim Henson died suddenly on May 16, 1990, at the age of fifty-three, from toxic shock syndrome caused by *Streptococcus pyogenes* — a bacterial infection that can be fatal within hours if untreated. He had felt ill for several days but delayed seeking medical attention. A healthy man in his early fifties, dead within forty-eight hours of being admitted to the hospital.

The Disney deal fell apart after Henson's death. His family retained control for the next fourteen years — the Henson children, running the company their father had built, making the decisions he did not live to make. They held the line. They kept the Muppets independent. They produced new work. They maintained the spirit. For fourteen years, they did what Joe Clokey did for eight: carried the living thing through a world that wanted to consume it.

In 2004, The Walt Disney Company acquired the Muppets and Bear in the Big Blue House from The Jim Henson Company for a reported $75 million — widely considered a fraction of the brand's true value. Seventy-five million for Kermit the Frog. For Miss Piggy. For Fozzie Bear, Gonzo, Animal, the Swedish Chef, Dr. Teeth, Scooter, Rowlf, Statler and Waldorf. Seventy-five million for a cultural legacy that had shaped two generations of American childhood. The price of a few mid-range apartments in Manhattan.

Disney now owns the Muppets entirely. The Henson family retained *Sesame Street*, *Fraggle Rock*, and the Henson creature shop, but the crown jewels — the characters that Jim Henson made with his own hands, that he performed with his own voice, that carried his own personality into millions of living rooms — belong to the Walt Disney Company, which is the largest entertainment conglomerate on earth, which has a market capitalization exceeding $150 billion, and which produces content the way a factory produces widgets: at scale, on schedule, optimized for return.

Jim Henson was fifty-three when he died. Joe Clokey was fifty-six. Will Vinton was seventy. All independent creators. All separated from their creations by forces beyond their control — financial leverage, institutional power, and in Henson's case, a death so sudden that no succession plan could have been executed. Joe Clokey told his son, months before his own death, that he was "being affected by them the same way Jim Henson was." He saw the parallel. He named it. He identified the pattern before the pattern took him.

The Jim Henson Company — run by Henson's children — later partnered with Joe Clokey's Premavision in 2015 to produce a new full stop-motion Gumby series faithful to Art Clokey's original vision. The children of two creators, both taken too soon, trying to bring both legacies back to the world. No co-production partner would fund it. The project died in silence. The children of the creators could not find a way through the gate, because the gate was held by the same architecture that had consumed their fathers' work.

## The Architecture

Four creators. Four properties. Four separations. The mechanism varies — hostile takeover, bankruptcy auction, financial audit retaliation, death — but the outcome is identical: the creator is displaced and the creation is absorbed into a larger entity that did not build it, does not understand it, and cannot reproduce the living quality that made it valuable.

This is not a conspiracy theory. A conspiracy requires coordinated secret action. What these cases reveal is something more structural: an *architecture* of consolidation that does not require coordination because it is built into the incentive structure of the industry itself.

The FCC's regulatory framework, from the Radio Act of 1927 through the Telecommunications Act of 1996, systematically concentrated broadcast distribution into fewer and fewer hands. Fifty major media companies in 1983 became six by 2012. Independent creators — no matter how talented, no matter how popular, no matter how culturally significant their work — cannot reach audiences without passing through the distribution bottleneck that these companies control.

The bottleneck is the mechanism. The leverage comes from controlling access to the audience. The creator needs the distributor more than the distributor needs the creator — because there are always more creators than there are distribution channels. This asymmetry creates the conditions for extraction: the creator builds something valuable, the distributor controls access to the market, and the eventual transfer of ownership is not a negotiation between equals but a capitulation by the weaker party.

Art Clokey saw this. He spent his entire life refusing to capitulate. Joe Clokey inherited the refusal and paid for it with eight years of closed doors and a death at fifty-six. Will Vinton refused and was fired. Phil Vischer refused and went bankrupt. Klasky and Csupo asked for an audit and lost their partnership. Jim Henson hesitated — and died before he could decide.

The pattern is not coincidence. It is architecture. And the architecture is the block — the geometric, rigid, stackable structure of institutional power that exists not to create but to *control* creation. To stand between the creator and the audience. To extract value from the living thing without being able to produce it.

The blocks cannot create Gumby. They cannot create VeggieTales. They cannot create Rugrats. They cannot create the Muppets. They can only acquire them — by leverage, by auction, by termination, by outliving the creator.

But the blocks cannot acquire the seed. The seed is too small, too distributed, too patient. The seed sits in the ash and waits for fire.

And the fire is coming.
