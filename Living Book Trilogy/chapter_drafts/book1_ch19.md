## Part 1: The Government-Created Monopoly

### How the Cartel Was Built

No private company can legally broadcast in the United States without a license from the Federal Communications Commission. This is not a free market — it is a government-granted monopoly on access to the public.

**The legal foundation:**
- The **Radio Act of 1927** claimed government ownership of the electromagnetic spectrum
- The **Communications Act of 1934** created the FCC to allocate broadcast licenses
- The FCC decides who can transmit and who cannot. Broadcasting without a license is a federal crime.

This system does not create competition. It creates a fixed number of permits distributed by a government agency. The permit holders become an artificial oligopoly by design.

**The consolidation:**

The **Telecommunications Act of 1996** — signed by President Clinton — was marketed as deregulation. It did not abolish the FCC or the license system. It removed the caps on how many licenses one company could hold while keeping the total number of licenses fixed. The result:

| Year | Number of major media companies controlling US media |
|------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| 1983 | ~50 |
| 1996 | ~10 |
| 2005 | **6** |

By 2005, five companies controlled approximately three-quarters of all prime-time television: **Viacom, Disney, Time Warner, News Corp,** and **NBC Universal.**

### What This Meant for Independent Creators

Before consolidation, an independent animation studio had multiple paths to an audience:
- **Syndication** — sell directly to independent local TV stations
- **Licensing** — license content to a network while retaining IP ownership
- **Home video** — distribute through independent video companies

After consolidation:
- **Syndication died** — independent stations were bought by the big 5
- **Networks demanded IP ownership** — Nickelodeon, Cartoon Network, Disney Channel, and Fox Kids all required creators to surrender ownership of their characters as a condition of airtime
- **Home video consolidated** — Blockbuster (Viacom) and Walmart controlled shelf space
- **Cable systems were owned by the same companies** making the content — vertical integration meant the producer, the network, the cable operator, and the merchandiser were the same entity

By the late 1990s, there were six doors to reach the American audience. The same people held all the keys. If you weren't inside one of these vertically integrated stacks, you had no path to distribution.

## Part 4: Art Clokey — The Man They Couldn't Control

### Who Art Clokey Was

Art Clokey (1921-2010) created Gumby in the 1950s. He was a pioneer of stop-motion clay animation and one of the most recognized figures in children's entertainment history. But he was also something the industry cartel could not tolerate: **a genuinely independent creator operating on moral principles.**

- **Refused merchandising for 8 years** after Gumby's creation: "I was a very idealistic person, and I didn't want to exploit children."
- Called Gumby **"an act of love to children."**
- Ran his own independent studio, **Premavision**, in Sausalito, California. Never network-owned. Never corporate-controlled.
- Was a spiritual seeker — seminary student, Buddhist, follower of Sai Baba. The documentary about him is titled *Gumby Dharma.*
- Also created *Davey and Goliath*, a Christian educational series for the Lutheran Church.
- **Trained more than half the animators** who worked on Tim Burton's *Nightmare Before Christmas*. His people went to Pixar and Disney. Art stayed independent.

Gumby's philosophy was embedded in its form:
- **Clay doesn't command. It becomes. It transforms.** Gumby moved through his world not as a rigid hero but as a living metaphor for creative freedom.
- **Green** — the color of life and growth — was deliberate. His form resembles a sprouting seedling: legs like roots, arms like branches, head leaning toward the sun.
- Gumby entered stories through the **portals of books** — imagination as a doorway, not a product.

From the cartel's perspective, Gumby was dangerous not because Art knew anything about their operations, but because of what he represented: **an independent, ethical, beloved children's property operating entirely outside their control.** If Gumby succeeds independently, it proves the system is unnecessary. The cartel's power depends on everyone believing there's no other way.

## Part 6: The Pattern Across Independent Creators

Gumby was not the only independent children's property destroyed by this system. The same cartel crushed every independent creator who refused to surrender.

Kricfalusi created Ren & Stimpy and sold it to Nickelodeon. After conflicts over content and creative control, Laybourne's Nickelodeon **fired him from his own show** in September 1992. Arlene Klasky and Gabor Csupo created Rugrats and other properties for Nickelodeon. Their shows generated **$3.7 billion in merchandise revenue** since 1997. The creators received a fraction. Bartlett created Hey Arnold! for Nickelodeon. He wanted the freedom to work with other networks. Nickelodeon demanded exclusivity. He refused. **He was forced out.** A planned second Hey Arnold! movie was shelved for years.

### Will Vinton — Claymation Itself

Will Vinton literally **trademarked the word "Claymation."** He built his Portland studio into a powerhouse (California Raisins, M&Ms commercials). In 1998, **Phil Knight** (Nike co-founder) invested $5 million. When the studio hit financial trouble, Knight leveraged the debt, stacked the board with Nike executives, and installed his son Travis Knight on the board. Within six months, **Vinton was fired from the studio he founded.** Knight took ownership of Vinton's entire body of work — the M&Ms, the California Raisins, and the legal trademark "Claymation" itself. The studio was rebranded as **Laika**, clay was thrown out, and replaced with 3D-printed puppets and CGI. Vinton sued for corporate nepotism. The case was dismissed. **Vinton died in 2018** — the same year as Joe Clokey.
