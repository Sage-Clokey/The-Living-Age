# Gumby and the Cartel: How a Government-Created Media Monopoly Sabotaged America's Most Independent Children's Property

---

## Thesis

Between 1992 and 2018, Gumby -- one of the most recognized characters in American culture, created by an independent animator who explicitly refused to exploit children -- was systematically denied distribution despite proven audience demand. The government-created monopoly on broadcasting ensured there was no alternative path. When the last Clokey who could protect the IP died at 56, the property was acquired, stripped of its original medium, philosophy, and independence.

---

## Part 1: The Government-Created Monopoly

### How the Cartel Was Built

No private company can legally broadcast in the United States without a license from the Federal Communications Commission. This is not a free market -- it is a government-granted monopoly on access to the public.

**The legal foundation:**
- The **Radio Act of 1927** claimed government ownership of the electromagnetic spectrum
- The **Communications Act of 1934** created the FCC to allocate broadcast licenses
- The FCC decides who can transmit and who cannot. Broadcasting without a license is a federal crime.

This system does not create competition. It creates a fixed number of permits distributed by a government agency. The permit holders become an artificial oligopoly by design.

**The consolidation:**

The **Telecommunications Act of 1996** -- signed by President Clinton -- was marketed as deregulation. It did not abolish the FCC or the license system. It removed the caps on how many licenses one company could hold while keeping the total number of licenses fixed. The result:

| Year | Number of major media companies controlling US media |
|------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| 1983 | ~50 |
| 1996 | ~10 |
| 2005 | **6** |

By 2005, five companies controlled approximately three-quarters of all prime-time television.

### What This Meant for Independent Creators

Before consolidation, an independent animation studio had multiple paths to an audience:
- **Syndication** -- sell directly to independent local TV stations
- **Licensing** -- license content to a network while retaining IP ownership
- **Home video** -- distribute through independent video companies

After consolidation:
- **Syndication died** -- independent stations were bought by the major corporations
- **Networks demanded IP ownership** -- every major children's network required creators to surrender ownership of their characters as a condition of airtime
- **Home video consolidated** -- a handful of corporations controlled shelf space
- **Cable systems were owned by the same companies** making the content -- vertical integration meant the producer, the network, the cable operator, and the merchandiser were the same entity

By the late 1990s, there were six doors to reach the American audience. The same people held all the keys. If you weren't inside one of these vertically integrated stacks, you had no path to distribution.

## Part 2: Art Clokey -- The Man They Couldn't Control

### Who Art Clokey Was

Art Clokey (1921-2010) created Gumby in the 1950s. He showed his thesis film *Gumbasia* to a movie studio, and they asked him to make a character out of it. He did -- and then he bought the character back, because he understood even then that the living thing had to stay in the hands of the person who understood what it was. He took Gumby to NBC, where the character found his audience.

He was a pioneer of stop-motion clay animation and one of the most recognized figures in children's entertainment history. But he was also something the industry cartel could not tolerate: **a genuinely independent creator operating on moral principles.**

- **Refused merchandising for 8 years** after Gumby's creation: "I was a very idealistic person, and I didn't want to exploit children."
- Called Gumby **"an act of love to children."**
- Ran his own independent studio, **Premavision**, in Sausalito, California. Never network-owned. Never corporate-controlled.
- Was a spiritual seeker -- seminary student, Buddhist, follower of Sai Baba. The documentary about him is titled *Gumby Dharma.*
- Also created *Davey and Goliath*, a Christian educational series for the Lutheran Church.
- **Trained more than half the animators** who worked on Tim Burton's *Nightmare Before Christmas*. His people went to Pixar and Disney. Art stayed independent.

Gumby's philosophy was embedded in its form:
- **Clay doesn't command. It becomes. It transforms.** Gumby moved through his world not as a rigid hero but as a living metaphor for creative freedom.
- **Green** -- the color of life and growth -- was deliberate. His form resembles a sprouting seedling: legs like roots, arms like branches, head leaning toward the sun.
- Gumby entered stories through the **portals of books** -- imagination as a doorway, not a product.

From the cartel's perspective, Gumby was dangerous not because Art knew anything about their operations, but because of what he represented: **an independent, ethical, beloved children's property operating entirely outside their control.** If Gumby succeeds independently, it proves the system is unnecessary. The cartel's power depends on everyone believing there's no other way.

## Part 3: The Sabotage -- A Chronological Case

### Phase 1: The Movie That Couldn't Get Distribution (1992-1995)

Art Clokey produced 99 new episodes of *Gumby Adventures* (1988-2002) from his studio in Sausalito. These aired as reruns on Nickelodeon and were **top-rated for three years** -- proving massive audience demand.

He completed *Gumby: The Movie* in **1992.**

**No major distributor would touch it for three years.**

- Not Paramount
- Not Disney
- Not Warner Bros.
- Not any of the major studios

The film was finally released in **1995** through **Arrow Releasing** -- a tiny independent distributor -- in just **21 theaters.** It grossed $57,100 against a $2.8 million budget.

But it sold approximately **1 million copies on VHS.**

One million families bought the movie when they could find it. The audience existed. The demand was proven. The only thing missing was access to the distribution system -- a system controlled by a small number of people who had already decided that an independent, uncompromisable creator would not be given access.

The same year, 1995: *Toy Story* released and CGI replaced claymation as the industry standard -- providing convenient cover for why Gumby "couldn't compete." But the distribution blockade preceded the CGI shift. The movie was finished in 1992 and sat on a shelf for three years. That wasn't a technology problem. That was a gatekeeping decision.

### Phase 2: The Deals That Collapsed (2000s-2017)

After Art Clokey died on January 8, 2010, his son **Joe Clokey** became president of Premavision/Clokey Productions. Joe spent the next eight years fighting to bring Gumby back through legitimate industry channels.

The pattern was consistent: deals would materialize, agreements would be reached, and then they would be **sat on or fall through for no apparent reason.**

**Deal 1: Disney (early 2000s)**
- A deal with Disney fell through. Details unknown.

**Deal 2: Classic Media (2006-2012)**
- In October 2006, Classic Media and Premavision announced a "long-term joint venture." Classic Media would manage US and international television, film, home video, digital, and promotional rights.
- **Tom Sarnoff** -- former West Coast EVP of NBC and the man who originally greenlit the Gumby Show on NBC in 1956 -- negotiated the deal for the Clokey side.
- The deal included plans for a **direct-to-video movie** (target release: 2008) and an **all-new TV series**, with Clokey Productions producing.
- **Neither was ever produced.** Six years. No movie. No series. Nothing.
- The license expired on **September 30, 2012.** Rights reverted to Premavision.
- In July 2012, DreamWorks Animation bought Classic Media for $155 million. In 2016, NBCUniversal bought DreamWorks. Gumby was no longer part of the catalog -- the Clokeys had kept ownership. But six years of potential production had been wasted.

- [DreamWorks Acquires Classic Media -- Deadline (2012)](https://deadline.com/2012/07/dreamworks-animation-buy-classic-media-rocky-bullwinkle-305403/)

**Deal 3: Jim Henson Company (2015)**
- In April 2015, the Jim Henson Company and Premavision announced a new **full stop-motion Gumby series** targeting fall 2016 delivery.
- This was the most faithful attempt to Art Clokey's vision -- classic Plasticine claymation, updated designs, stories about friendship and courage.
- The project needed a **co-production partner** to fund it.
- **No partner materialized.** The project died silently. No follow-up announcement was ever made.
- By 2015, the six major media companies controlled virtually all children's entertainment funding and distribution. An independent co-production partner with the resources to fund a children's series essentially did not exist anymore.

**Deal 4: Amazon (~2017)**
- A deal with Amazon fell through. Details unknown.
- This was Joe Clokey's last known major attempt.

**Papercutz Comics (2017)**
- A Gumby comic series launched in July 2017 through Papercutz.
- **Cancelled after 3 issues.**

### Joe's Own Words

A few months before his death, Joe Clokey told his son Sage that he was **"being affected by them the same way Jim Henson was."**

This is a firsthand statement from Joe himself. He identified a "them." He connected his own situation to Henson's. He saw the pattern before he died.

**Jim Henson died on May 16, 1990, at age 53.** Official cause: toxic shock syndrome from a sudden streptococcal infection. A healthy man in his early 50s, dead within 48 hours of hospital admission. He had been in active negotiations to sell the Muppets to Disney. The deal collapsed after his death -- it took 14 years before Disney finally acquired the Muppets in 2004.

Henson, like Clokey, was an independent children's creator operating outside the corporate system. The Muppets, like Gumby, were a beloved property held by their creator. And the Jim Henson Company -- run by his children after his death -- was the same company that partnered with Joe Clokey in 2015 for the Gumby reboot that no co-production partner would fund.

| | **Jim Henson** | **Joe Clokey** |
|---|---|---|
| **Property** | Muppets | Gumby |
| **Status** | Independent creator | Independent IP holder |
| **Age at death** | 53 | 56 |
| **Cause** | Sudden infection -> organ failure | Brain bleed -> cardiac arrest |
| **Speed** | Days | One month |
| **Deal at time of death** | Disney acquisition in progress | Deals repeatedly collapsing |
| **After death** | IP eventually went to Disney (2004) | IP sold (2022) |
| **Pattern** | Healthy man in his 50s, sudden onset, dead within weeks | Healthy man in his 50s, sudden onset, dead within weeks |

Joe saw this parallel himself. He told his son. Months later, he was dead.

### Phase 3: Joe Clokey's Death (2018)

- **Onset:** February 2, 2018
- **Death:** March 2, 2018 -- one month from first symptoms to death
- **Cause:** Brain bleed leading to cardiac arrest
- **Age:** 56

Joe Clokey was 56 years old. He was the last person standing between Gumby and the cartel. Every deal he pursued had been killed from above.

Public reports listed cardiac arrest as the cause of death. The family's knowledge is that it was a **brain bleed that led to cardiac arrest** -- the brain bleed was the actual initiating event.

### Phase 4: The Acquisition (2022)

- **February 2022:** The entire Gumby universe was acquired from the Clokey estate.
- All rights: Premavision, Prematoy, film, television, streaming, consumer products, licensing, publishing -- everything.
- The acquiring corporation commissioned *Gumby Kids* -- **2D animation. Not claymation.** An adult animated version is also in development.

While the Clokeys were alive and holding the full IP, Gumby couldn't get through the door. After Joe's death, the IP was sold. And it is now being stripped of everything Art Clokey built -- no clay, no stop-motion, no philosophy of transformation and freedom.

And then they made him a city manager.

Look at what Art Clokey created. Gumby is shapeshifting clay -- the most living, adaptive, decentralized material a creator has ever worked with. Clay doesn't hold rigid form. It responds to the hand. It becomes whatever it touches. Gumby walked through the portals of books and became part of the story. He was green -- the color of life, of growth, of something sprouting. His body looked like a seedling: legs like roots, arms like branches, head leaning toward the sun. He was not a character who commanded his world. He was a character who *became* his world. He was the spiral -- living order, transformation, creative freedom made flesh. Made clay.

They made him the central planner.

They took the most living character in American animation -- a being whose entire identity is that he bends, morphs, enters stories, and transforms -- and they put him behind a desk. They gave him a title. They made him manage a city. They made Gumby into the block.

This is not a creative decision. This is a confession. They looked at a character who embodies decentralized, adaptive, living freedom -- and the only thing they could imagine doing with him was making him run a bureaucracy. Because that is the only kind of order they understand. They cannot see the spiral. They can only see the block. They cannot see what clay *is* -- they see a green rectangle and think "IP we can slot into a structure."

Art Clokey called Gumby "an act of love to children." They turned him into a middle manager. The distance between those two visions is the distance between everything this book is about.

And here is the part that would be funny if it weren't so revealing: Gumby already had villains who represented exactly what is being done to him. **The Blockheads.** G and J. Red, rigid, geometric blocks. They couldn't transform. They couldn't enter stories. They couldn't become anything other than what they were -- angular, uniform, fixed. They existed to oppose everything Gumby represented. Art Clokey literally built the antagonist of his own story around the shape of centralized rigidity -- the block against the clay, the dead form against the living form.

The Blockheads were never meant to win. Not because they weren't strong -- but because blocks are rigid, and rigid things break. Time does not spare what cannot bend. Clay survives the earthquake because clay transforms. The block cracks. That is not a prediction. That is geometry.

## Part 4: The Pattern Across Independent Creators

Gumby was not the only independent children's property destroyed by this system. The same cartel crushed every independent creator who refused to surrender.

### John Kricfalusi -- Ren & Stimpy

Kricfalusi created Ren & Stimpy and sold it to Nickelodeon. After conflicts over content and creative control, Nickelodeon **fired him from his own show** in September 1992. The network owned the IP. They ran three more seasons without the creator. Kricfalusi was left without his characters.

### Klasky Csupo -- Rugrats

Arlene Klasky and Gabor Csupo created Rugrats and other properties for Nickelodeon. Their shows generated **$3.7 billion in merchandise revenue** since 1997. The creators received a fraction. When they hired auditors to examine the corporate books, **the network terminated the partnership entirely.** Klasky Csupo went dormant for four years.

### Craig Bartlett -- Hey Arnold!

Bartlett created Hey Arnold! for Nickelodeon. He wanted the freedom to work with other networks. The network demanded exclusivity. He refused. **He was forced out.** A planned second Hey Arnold! movie was shelved for years.

### Will Vinton -- Claymation Itself

Will Vinton literally **trademarked the word "Claymation."** He built his Portland studio into a powerhouse (California Raisins, M&Ms commercials). In 1998, **Phil Knight** (Nike co-founder) invested $5 million. When the studio hit financial trouble, Knight leveraged the debt, stacked the board with Nike executives, and installed his son Travis Knight on the board. Within six months, **Vinton was fired from the studio he founded.** Knight took ownership of Vinton's entire body of work -- the M&Ms, the California Raisins, and the legal trademark "Claymation" itself. The studio was rebranded as **Laika**, clay was thrown out, and replaced with 3D-printed puppets and CGI. Vinton sued for corporate nepotism. The case was dismissed. **Vinton died in 2018** -- the same year as Joe Clokey.

### The Pattern

| Creator | Property | What They Built | How It Was Taken |
|---------|----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Art Clokey** | Gumby | 57 years of independent production | Denied distribution; IP sold after family died |
| **John Kricfalusi** | Ren & Stimpy | Created the show | Fired; network kept the IP |
| **Klasky Csupo** | Rugrats | $3.7B in merchandise | Partnership terminated when they questioned the money |
| **Craig Bartlett** | Hey Arnold! | Created the show | Forced out for wanting creative freedom |
| **Will Vinton** | Claymation (the word itself) | Pioneered the medium | Hostile takeover; fired from his own studio |

Every independent creator who resisted the system was fired, starved, bought out, or taken over. The creators who cooperated -- who surrendered ownership and didn't ask questions -- survived. This is not a market. It is a protection racket.

## Part 5: The Last Independent Children's IP

Gumby was not just one of many independent children's properties that got absorbed. By the time it was acquired in 2022, it was **the last major American children's character still held by its creator's family as a small, independent operation.**

### The Consolidation of Children's IP

Every other classic children's property had already been absorbed into corporate hands:

| Property | Creator | Who Owns It Now | How It Was Taken |
|----------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Thomas the Tank Engine** | Rev. W. Awdry (d. 1997) | **Mattel** -- bought HiT Entertainment for $680M (2012) | Sold through chain of corporate acquisitions |
| **Peanuts** | Charles Schulz (d. 2000) | **Sony** -- controlling stake (2023) | Family retained ~20%, Sony controls |
| **Curious George** | H.A. & Margret Rey (both deceased) | **NBCUniversal / Houghton Mifflin** | Estate lost control through corporate licensing |
| **Paddington** | Michael Bond (d. 2017) | **WildBrain / StudioCanal** | Licensing company acquired commercial rights |
| **VeggieTales** | Phil Vischer | **NBCUniversal** -- bought at bankruptcy auction for $19M (2003) | Creator's company went bankrupt; lost everything |
| **Peppa Pig** | Neville Astley & Mark Baker | **Hasbro** -- $4 billion acquisition of eOne (2019) | Corporate buyout |
| **Cocomelon** | Independent creator | **Candle Media (Blackstone-backed)** -- via Moonbug acquisition | YouTube success -> corporate acquisition |

### The VeggieTales Parallel

Phil Vischer's story is the clearest parallel to what happened to the Clokeys:

- Vischer created VeggieTales in 1993 through his company Big Idea Productions
- It became one of the best-selling children's video franchises of the late 1990s
- The company overextended, partly due to a failed distribution lawsuit
- **Big Idea filed for bankruptcy in 2003**
- **Classic Media** -- the same company that held the Gumby license from 2006-2012 and produced nothing -- bought VeggieTales at the bankruptcy auction for $19 million
- Classic Media was later absorbed into DreamWorks Classics, then into NBCUniversal
- Vischer lost his creation. He's spoken publicly about the pain of watching someone else control what he built.

Classic Media touched both properties. Produced nothing with either one.

### What This Means

When Gumby was acquired in 2022, the consolidation of American children's entertainment IP into corporate hands was complete. There is no longer a single major, broadly recognized, classic American children's character operated by its creator's family as a small independent business.

Every one of them is now inside the corporate stack -- owned by Mattel, Sony, NBCUniversal, Hasbro, WildBrain, or Blackstone-backed holding companies. The same vertical integration that the Telecommunications Act enabled in broadcasting has now captured the IP itself. The corporations don't just control the distribution anymore. They own the characters.

Gumby was the last one standing.

## A Note on What Cannot Be Gatekept

Art Clokey needed their permission to reach an audience. He didn't get it.

His grandson doesn't need their permission.

The internet -- YouTube, GitHub, open-source, podcasting, Substack -- is the closest thing to an actual free market in media distribution that has ever existed. No FCC license required. No gatekeeper approval. No network ownership of your IP.

Art shaped clay into a living metaphor for freedom. His grandson is building technology that designs with living organisms. The medium changed. The message didn't.

The Living Age cannot be gatekept. It is open-source. It is on GitHub. The philosophy is encoded in the technology itself. There is no deal to collapse, no license to expire, no single person whose death transfers the IP to the cartel.

That's the design.
