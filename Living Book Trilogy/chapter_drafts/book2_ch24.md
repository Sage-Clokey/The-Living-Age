# The Sokovia Problem: Why Morality Cannot Be Centralized

**What Captain America understood about ethics boards that bioethicists don't.**

*By Sage Clokey*

---

## The Accords

In *Captain America: Civil War*, the United Nations drafts the Sokovia Accords — a legal framework to place the Avengers under governmental oversight.

The justification is collateral damage. Sokovia leveled. Lagos in flames. Innocent people dead in the wake of extraordinary individuals acting on their own judgment. The world watches the footage and demands control. A panel. A committee. A board that decides when the Avengers may act, where they may go, and what they may do.

Steve Rogers refuses to sign.

> *"The safest hands are still our own."*

Tony Stark pushes back. Accountability matters, he says. People died.

And Rogers doesn't deny it. He carries it. But he sees something Stark doesn't — or won't. The question is not whether mistakes were made. The question is who you trust to do better next time. The individual conscience, or the institution.

Rogers answers the only way he can:

> *"What if there's somewhere we need to go and they won't let us? What if there's somewhere we don't want to go and they make us?"*

That is not arrogance. It is the recognition of something most people feel but few are willing to say out loud: a centralized authority that controls moral action does not eliminate evil. It merely relocates it — and makes it harder to resist.

This is the insight the block civilization cannot tolerate, because it strikes at the very foundation of the block's claim to legitimacy. Every empire, every bureaucracy, every centralized system of power justifies its existence by the same argument: without us, there is only chaos. Without oversight, there is only recklessness. Without the committee, there is only the individual — and the individual cannot be trusted. Rogers does not dispute that individuals make mistakes. He disputes the premise that institutions make fewer of them. And the record — the historical, documented, blood-soaked record — is on his side.

## The Arsonist Becomes the Fire Chief

Here is the detail the Accords' defenders never address.

Sokovia was destroyed by Ultron. And Ultron was not the product of rogue individuals acting without oversight. Ultron emerged from the same ecosystem of institutional power — SHIELD, Hydra nested inside SHIELD, government-funded weapons programs, intelligence agencies operating in the dark.

The entire infrastructure that enabled the catastrophe was institutional. The system didn't fail to prevent the disaster. The system *produced* it. And then the system turned around and demanded the authority to regulate the people who tried to stop it.

The pattern is not unique to fiction. It is, in fact, the defining pattern of centralized moral authority throughout history.

**Tuskegee (1932–1972).** The United States Public Health Service — a government institution — deliberately withheld syphilis treatment from Black men for forty years. Not rogue scientists. The institution itself, conducting the experiment by design. The men were told they were receiving free healthcare. They were told they were being treated. They were neither. The institution lied to them for four decades, and when the experiment was finally exposed, the institutional response was not contrition but damage control. The apparatus of oversight had not merely failed to prevent the atrocity. The apparatus of oversight was the atrocity.

**Forced Sterilization (1907–1970s).** Over sixty thousand Americans were forcibly sterilized under state laws upheld by the Supreme Court, where Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote in *Buck v. Bell* that "three generations of imbeciles are enough." The government did not fail to regulate. The government was the perpetrator. The highest court in the land reviewed the evidence, deliberated with the full weight of institutional authority, and concluded that the state had the right to sterilize citizens against their will. This was not a breakdown of the system. This was the system working exactly as designed — concentrating moral authority at the top and wielding it against the bodies of those at the bottom.

**Unit 731 (1937–1945).** The Imperial Japanese Army ran biological warfare experiments on living prisoners — under full state authority and institutional funding. Vivisection without anesthesia. Deliberate infection with plague and cholera. The experiments were not hidden from the military hierarchy. They were authorized by it. The institution did not fail to notice. The institution signed the requisition forms.

**Operation Paperclip (1945–1959).** After the war, the United States didn't prosecute those scientists. It recruited them. Nazi researchers who had conducted concentration camp experiments were brought into American labs, given security clearances and government salaries. The institution didn't punish the evil. It hired it. It calculated that the knowledge gained through atrocity was too valuable to waste on justice, and it made the calculation without consulting the conscience of anyone who might have said no. This is what centralized moral authority does with moral questions: it weighs them on a scale that has power on one side and principle on the other, and power is heavier every time.

**MKUltra (1953–1973).** The CIA conducted illegal mind-control experiments on unwitting American citizens. Authorized at the highest levels. Covered up for decades. When the program was finally exposed, the institutional response was to destroy as many documents as possible. The oversight mechanisms that were supposed to prevent such programs had not prevented them. The oversight mechanisms had been bypassed, co-opted, or simply ignored — because the people who controlled the institution also controlled the mechanisms that were supposed to constrain them.

Every case follows the same arc. The institution commits the atrocity, then positions itself as the necessary guardian against future atrocities. The arsonist becomes the fire chief. And anyone who questions the arrangement is told they are being irresponsible.

Rogers sees it for what it is. He is not claiming to be perfect. He is claiming something simpler and more radical: that an imperfect free person acting on conscience is less dangerous than an imperfect institution acting on power.

## The Ethics Board Problem

Now bring this to the present.

In bioethics — particularly in fields like brain organoid research, genetic engineering, and human subjects experimentation — the standard answer to moral complexity is the ethics board. The Institutional Review Board. The oversight committee. The regulatory body.

The assumption is always the same: individuals cannot be trusted with moral judgment, so we must centralize that judgment in a panel of appointed experts operating under institutional authority.

But ask the question Captain America asks. *Who sits on that board?*

People appointed by the same institutions with histories of moral catastrophe. People funded by the same grant structures that incentivize compliance over conscience. People whose careers depend on not disrupting the institutional order that employs them.

The ethics board does not eliminate bias. It institutionalizes bias and gives it the authority of consensus.

Consider what happens in practice. A researcher encounters a moral question — a question about whether a particular experiment on human tissue crosses a line that should not be crossed. If the researcher follows their conscience and refuses to proceed, they are labeled difficult, uncooperative, a poor team player. Their funding is at risk. Their career trajectory bends downward. But if they submit the question to the ethics board — if they outsource their conscience to the committee — and the committee says proceed, then the researcher is absolved. The moral weight has been transferred from the individual to the institution. And the institution, having no conscience of its own, carries that weight without feeling it.

A researcher who refuses to comply with an ethics board is labeled reckless. An ethics board that fails to prevent harm is labeled "imperfect but necessary." The individual bears full accountability. The institution bears none.

That is not oversight. That is the consolidation of moral authority into the hands of the very structures least equipped to wield it honestly.

The deeper problem is structural. An ethics board, by its nature, tends toward permission rather than prohibition. The board exists within an institution that funds research. The institution that funds research benefits from research being conducted. The board members are drawn from the same professional community that conducts the research. The incentives all flow in one direction: toward approval. The board that consistently blocks research will be reformed, restaffed, or replaced by one that does not. The board that consistently approves research will be praised for its efficiency and its understanding of the complexities involved. The structure selects for compliance. And compliance dressed in the language of ethics is the most dangerous form of moral abdication, because it looks like responsibility while functioning as its opposite.

## Every Person a Moral Node

There is another architecture.

One that does not concentrate moral authority but distributes it. One that does not ask "who should be in charge of ethics?" but instead recognizes that every person already is.

Think of it like a network. Not a hierarchy with a central server issuing commands, but a distributed system — every node processing, every node accountable, every node sovereign.

In this architecture, every researcher is a moral agent. Not a subordinate waiting for a committee's permission slip, but a person who must look at what they are doing and decide, on their own conscience, whether it is right.

Every community is a check — not through legal coercion, but through reputation, trust, and the natural consequences of acting in ways that violate the moral intuitions of the people around you.

And every individual receives their moral instructions not from a board, not from a government, not from an institution, but from something higher. Call it God. Call it the Creator. Call it the moral law written into the structure of reality itself.

The source of moral authority is not human bureaucracy. It is transcendent. It precedes the institution and will outlast it.

This is the architecture Steve Rogers lives by. He does not claim to be his own highest authority. He submits to something — to principle, to the bone-deep conviction that some things are right and some things are wrong and no vote of a panel can make it otherwise.

But that something is not the UN. It is not a committee. It is the still, stubborn knowledge held at the level of the individual soul.

The distributed model does not mean there are no consequences for immoral action. It means the consequences are natural rather than artificial — they flow from the web of relationships in which every person is embedded, not from the decree of a distant authority that has never met the people it governs. A researcher who violates the trust of their community loses that trust. A scientist who crosses a line that their peers recognize as wrong faces the judgment not of a committee that meets quarterly but of every colleague who knows them, every student who watched them, every person whose respect they earned and then betrayed. This is accountability that operates at the speed of relationship, not the speed of bureaucracy. It is messier. It is less predictable. And it is incomparably more honest.

## The Creator's Design Language

This is not just philosophy. The architecture of creation itself confirms it.

A living organism is not a top-down command structure. It is a distributed network — trillions of cells, each carrying the full genome, each making local decisions, each responding to signals from its neighbors.

There is no central planning committee in the body telling the liver what to do and the heart when to beat. There is a shared code — DNA — and each cell reads that code and acts on it in its own context.

The immune system does not wait for approval from a board. It recognizes a threat and it responds. If it waited for centralized authorization, you would die of infection before the memo cleared committee review.

This is the Creator's design language: **distributed intelligence, local moral agency, shared foundational code.**

The whole organism thrives not because a central authority manages every cell, but because every cell carries the instructions within itself and has the freedom to act on them.

When the immune system malfunctions — when it attacks the body's own tissue, as in autoimmune disease — the problem is not that the cells lack a central authority. The problem is that the local intelligence has been corrupted. The solution is not to impose more centralized control. The solution is to restore the integrity of the local decision-making. To heal the node, not to override it. The medical analogy is precise: autoimmune disease is not cured by giving the body a dictator. It is cured by restoring the distributed intelligence that allows each cell to correctly distinguish self from threat.

The Sokovia Accords are the biological equivalent of telling the immune system it needs UN approval before it can fight a virus. It sounds reasonable in a press conference. In practice, it kills the organism.

## The Safest Hands

The argument for centralized ethics boards rests on a premise that feels intuitive but collapses under the weight of every century that has tested it: that concentrating moral authority in institutions makes the world safer.

History says otherwise. Fiction illuminates what history proves.

Captain America does not reject accountability. He rejects the lie that accountability flows downward from institutions to individuals.

True accountability flows upward — from each person to the moral law above them — and outward — from each person to the people beside them. It is not imposed. It is chosen. It is not enforced from the top down. It is grown from within.

This is the architecture the Sagent Creed describes. Not the abolition of responsibility but its radical redistribution — from the committee to the conscience, from the boardroom to the individual, from the institution that claims authority to the person who actually carries the weight. The Sagent does not wait for permission to do what is right. The Sagent does not submit their conscience to a panel. The Sagent stands before the moral law — the one written on the heart, the one that no legislature can repeal and no committee can amend — and answers to that law alone.

Every person is a node. Every conscience is sovereign. Every soul answers to the Creator, not the committee.

The safest hands are still our own.

> *"The price of freedom is high. It always has been. And it's a price I'm willing to pay."*

Not because freedom guarantees perfect outcomes. It does not. The individual conscience will sometimes err. The free person will sometimes make the wrong call. But the error of a free conscience is self-correcting — because the person who made the mistake still has the capacity to recognize it, to feel its weight, to change course. The error of an institution is self-reinforcing — because the institution that made the mistake has every incentive to defend it, to justify it, to build policy around it, to make the mistake permanent so that admitting it becomes impossible.

The safest hands are not the ones that never make mistakes. The safest hands are the ones that can still feel what they are holding.
