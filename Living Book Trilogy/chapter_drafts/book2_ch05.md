# Chapter 5: Who Owns Your Life?

---

## The Two Answers

There are two possible answers to this question. Only two. Every political philosophy, every economic system, every argument about rights and obligations and the proper scope of government — all of it reduces to which answer you give.

Either you own your life, or someone else does.

Everything follows from there. Every debate about taxation, about bodily autonomy, about conscription, about licensing, about regulation, about the proper limits of state power — every one of these debates is a downstream consequence of which answer you accept to this single question. Get this question right and the rest falls into place with the force of logic. Get it wrong and you spend your life arguing over symptoms while the disease goes unnamed.

---

## The Intuition

Ask anyone on the street — anyone, of any political persuasion, any background, any nation — and they will give you the same first answer. Of course I own my life. Who else would? The question seems almost absurd in its obviousness. Self-ownership is so deeply intuitive that most people have never even articulated it as a principle. They simply live it. They make choices about what to eat, where to work, whom to love, what to believe. They experience their body as their own. They experience their mind as their own. They experience their labor — the creative output of their body and mind working together — as their own.

This intuition is correct. And it is the most dangerous idea in the history of political power.

Because if you own yourself — if you are the sovereign proprietor of your own body, your own mind, your own labor, your own life — then certain things follow with the force of gravity. They follow not because a philosopher argued them into existence, but because they are logically inescapable once the premise is accepted. The premise is a seed. And the tree that grows from it has branches that reach into every corner of political and economic life — branches that the powerful would very much prefer you never notice.

If you own yourself, you own the product of your labor. The work of your hands, the output of your mind, the value you create through the application of your time and energy — these belong to you. Not because the state grants you property rights. Not because a legal framework permits you to keep what you earn. Because the labor is an extension of the self, and the self belongs to you. The artist owns the painting because the painting is the physical expression of the artist's time, vision, and skill — and if the artist owns none of those things, then the artist is not an artist but a tool, and someone else is the artist using them.

If you own yourself, no one has the right to your body without your consent. No one may inject you with a substance you did not choose. No one may prevent you from ingesting a substance you did choose. No one may compel you to labor for their benefit. No one may claim a portion of your existence as their due. These are not political positions. They are logical consequences of a premise that every person on earth already accepts intuitively. The six-year-old who says "that's mine" when someone grabs their toy is making a property claim rooted in self-ownership. The adult who says "it's my body" when asked about a medical decision is making the same claim. The worker who says "I earned this" when looking at a paycheck is making the same claim. The claim is universal. It is pre-political. It is older than any government and more fundamental than any constitution.

If you own yourself, your life is not a resource to be managed by an external authority. It is a sovereign domain. And any claim on it — any claim at all, by any institution, for any purpose — requires your actual, specific, revocable consent.

This is the foundation. It is simple. It is obvious. And the entire architecture of the modern state is built on its denial.

---

## The Theft With Better Branding

Consider your paycheck.

You worked for it. You traded hours of your life — hours you will never recover, hours subtracted permanently from the finite sum of time you have on this earth — for a number. That number represents the value your employer agreed your labor was worth, and you accepted the terms freely. The exchange was voluntary. The consent was mutual. The transaction was just.

Before that number reaches you, a portion is removed. You did not negotiate the percentage. You did not agree to the deduction. You were not given the option to decline. The institution that takes this portion was not party to the original agreement between you and your employer. It inserted itself into the transaction by force — not the overt force of a drawn weapon, but the structural force of a system that will cage you if you refuse. The weapon is there. It is simply kept in a drawer, behind a desk, in a building you have never visited, waiting for the moment you decline to comply.

Call it what it is.

If a man on the street takes your wallet at gunpoint, we call it robbery. If he takes a portion of your paycheck every two weeks under threat of imprisonment, we call it — what? Taxation? Civic duty? The price of living in a society?

The name changes. The act does not.

The objection comes immediately, and it sounds like this: but taxes pay for roads, for schools, for hospitals, for defense. Without taxes, who would provide these things?

The objection assumes its own conclusion. It assumes that the only way to fund roads and schools and hospitals is through compulsory extraction. It assumes that voluntary cooperation — the same voluntary cooperation that built every mutual aid society, every community fire brigade, every barn-raising, every open-source project, every charity, every church, every freely given act of human solidarity in the history of the species — is insufficient. It assumes that human beings, left free, will not provide for their own communities. And this assumption — examine it closely — is not a neutral observation about human nature. It is the founding myth of the institution that benefits from the assumption. The state tells you that without the state, nothing works. This is like the casino telling you that without the casino, no one would know how to play cards.

This assumption is not supported by history. It is not supported by economics. It is not supported by the evidence of your own life, in which the overwhelming majority of the good things — the meals shared with friends, the neighbors who watch your house, the stranger who changes your tire, the community that rallies when disaster strikes — were provided voluntarily, without compulsion, without a government agent standing behind the giver with a gun.

The assumption that compulsion is necessary is the Block's founding myth. It is the jar-shaker's justification for the jar. And it persists not because it is true, but because the people inside the jar have been inside it so long that they cannot imagine the sky.

---

## Partial Ownership Is a Contradiction

There is no such thing as partial self-ownership that is not simply a different word for slavery.

This is the statement that makes people uncomfortable, and it should. Because once you follow the logic, there is no resting place between full self-ownership and full subjugation — only degrees of the same condition.

If another party has the right to thirty percent of your labor, you are thirty percent enslaved. The percentage may be negotiable. The master may be benevolent. The cage may be gilded. But the structure is the same: another party claims a portion of your existence as their own, and enforces that claim with force. Call it progressive taxation. Call it social insurance. Call it the common good. The names are different. The structure is identical. Someone else is claiming a portion of your life, and you did not agree to the claim, and the claim is enforced with violence. The slave who is allowed to keep seventy percent of the crop is still a slave. The percentage is a detail. The principle is the thing.

If a large group votes to take it — does the vote change the nature of the act? If fifty-one percent of the population votes to enslave the other forty-nine percent, is the slavery moral? The numbers do not transform coercion into consent. A million people demanding your wallet are not more legitimate than one. They are simply more dangerous. The act of voting does not create moral authority. It creates political power — which is a different thing entirely, and the conflation of the two is one of the most effective confidence tricks in the history of governance.

Democracy, in its popular conception, is the belief that the majority has the right to override the minority. But rights are not created by votes. The right to your own life exists before any ballot is cast, before any constitution is written, before any government is formed. It exists because you exist. And no process — no election, no legislature, no constitutional convention — can create the right to take what was never offered.

If you own your body, should you be allowed to put whatever you want into it? The question answers itself. If you own a house, may you paint the walls any color you wish? The owner decides. That is what ownership means. The moment someone else tells you what you may and may not put in your own body — what substances, what foods, what medicines, what chemicals — they are asserting that your body is not fully yours. That you are a tenant, and they are the landlord. That your self-ownership is conditional, revocable, subject to the approval of a party you did not invite into the relationship. And the landlord's justification is always the same: it is for your own good. The slave master said the same thing. The colonial governor said the same thing. Every person who has ever claimed authority over another person's body has said the same thing: I know better than you what is good for you. And the claim has been wrong every time — not because the claimant was always foolish, but because the claim itself violates something more fundamental than any policy argument. It violates the sovereignty of the self.

## The Sacred Claim

There is a deeper layer here, and it matters.

The Sagent Creed holds that human beings are owned — but not by other human beings. Not by institutions. Not by governments or kings or majorities.

You were made to be ruled. This statement is true — but not in the way the world has taught it. You were not made to be ruled by other humans. You were not made to be ruled by institutions, empires, or systems. You were made to live under the rule of the Creator alone — the one who made you, who knows the number of hairs on your head, who wrote the law on your heart before any legislature convened.

There can only be one master. And if the master is God — if the ultimate claim on your life belongs to the one who gave it — then no secondary claim is legitimate. No human being is born with the right to rule another. No institution inherits a right that no individual possesses. No vote can transfer what was never anyone's to give. The logic is simple and it is devastating: if no individual has the right to take your wallet by force, then a group of individuals does not acquire that right by voting on it. Rights are not created by aggregation. A thousand people have no more right to your life than one person does. The mathematics of coercion do not improve with scale.

This is not a theocratic argument. It is a liberating one. Because the claim that you belong to God is precisely the claim that you do not belong to Caesar. It is the oldest anti-authoritarian declaration in human history: we must obey God rather than men. Not as rebels. As people who know where sovereignty actually resides. The claim is not that you should submit to a different ruler. The claim is that the only ruler whose claim is just does not rule by force. Does not rule by extraction. Does not rule by coercion or fear or the threat of cages.

Humans are free because they are owned by the only owner whose claim is just. And that owner does not rule by force. That owner rules by love — by the still, quiet voice of conscience that tells you what is right before any law is written, that tells you who you are before any institution assigns you a role, that tells you what you are worth before any market prices your labor. The conscience is the voice of the owner. And the owner's instruction is always the same: you are free. You are sovereign. You belong to me, and because you belong to me, no one else can claim you. My ownership is your liberation.

## The Weight of the Question

Who gets to override the owner of a life — and by what right?

This is the question that unravels empires. Not because it is radical in the sense of being extreme, but because it is radical in the original sense — from the Latin radix, root. It goes to the root. And the root of every system of political authority, when you dig deep enough, is a claim of ownership over human lives.

The king claimed it by divine right. The state claims it by social contract. The majority claims it by democratic mandate. The claims differ in their language and their sophistication, but the structure is identical: we have the right to a portion of your life, and we will enforce that right with violence.

The Sagent's answer is simple, and it is the same answer that every conscience gives when it is honest with itself: no. You do not. My life belongs to me — and to the one who gave it. Not to you. Not to your institution. Not to your majority. The burden of proof is not on me to justify my freedom. The burden of proof is on you to justify your claim on it.

And that burden has never been met. Not by any king. Not by any parliament. Not by any constitution. Not by any theory of social contract or democratic legitimacy or utilitarian calculus. The burden has never been met because it cannot be met — because the claim is false, and no amount of argument can make a false claim true. You cannot argue your way to a right that does not exist. You can only enforce it — which brings us back to where we started, which is force, which is the mugger with better branding.

## The Duty That Remains

Self-ownership does not mean selfishness. This is the Block's most effective slander against freedom — the accusation that if people are left free, they will care only for themselves. That without compulsion, there is no compassion. That without taxation, there is no charity. That without the state, there is no solidarity.

The accusation is backwards.

It is every person's duty to uphold what is right in all they do. This must always be the highest priority — never to be sacrificed for power, wealth, or fame. Each person must stand firm in their convictions, refusing to cower before false authority. This is not a duty imposed from outside. It is a duty that arises from within — from the conscience that is the owner's voice, from the moral law written on the heart, from the recognition that self-ownership carries self-responsibility the way a river carries its current.

Self-ownership means self-responsibility. The person who owns their life bears the full weight of their choices. There is no institution to hide behind, no collective to absorb the blame, no system to offload the moral burden. You own your life. Therefore you answer for it. Not to the state. Not to the majority. To the truth written on your heart and to the God who wrote it.

And it turns out — as history demonstrates over and over — that people who bear the full weight of their own choices are more generous, more compassionate, more creative, and more courageous than people whose moral agency has been outsourced to an institution. The volunteer gives more than the taxpayer. The neighbor helps more than the bureaucrat. The free person builds more than the managed one. This is not sentimentality. It is the observable record. People who are free to choose generosity choose it more often and more meaningfully than people who are compelled to fund generosity through an institution they did not choose and cannot hold accountable.

This is not a theory. This is the record. Every great act of human solidarity — every barn raised, every stranger sheltered, every community rebuilt after disaster — was voluntary. The force was never what made us good. The force was what convinced us we could not be good without it.

---

*Either you own your life, or someone else does. There is no middle ground that is not a euphemism for captivity. The intuition that tells you your life is yours is not naive — it is the oldest truth you carry. And the systems that deny it do not do so because they are wiser than your conscience. They do so because your conscience, fully trusted, would make them unnecessary.*

*Who owns your life? You already know. The question is whether you will live as though you believe it.*
