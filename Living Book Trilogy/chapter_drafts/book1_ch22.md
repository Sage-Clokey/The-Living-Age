# Chapter 22: The Blue to the Green

## A Sagent's Testimony

---

## The State Chair

On April 1st, 2025, I became a state chair for Young Americans for Liberty.

YAL. The largest libertarian youth organization in the country. Thousands of members, hundreds of campuses, a professional operation with national calls, regional directors, performance quotas, talking points. My territory was California — specifically, the Santa Cruz area. My job was to manage college chapters at UC Santa Cruz, which meant facilitating meetings on a campus that was not set up for them, recruiting students who were more interested in surfing than political organizing, and making eighty phone calls every other week to people who mostly did not answer.

The tools of the job were spreadsheets. Call logs. Attendance trackers. Performance metrics piped into shared documents that regional directors reviewed on biweekly calls. The entire apparatus of modern political organizing — the same apparatus used by every campaign, every advocacy group, every institutional effort to convert human beings into measurable units of political output — compressed into rows and columns on a screen. Eighty calls. How many answered. How many committed. How many showed up. The spreadsheet did not care why they showed up. The spreadsheet did not measure whether the conversation changed anything inside them. The spreadsheet measured throughput, the way a factory measures widgets per hour, and the state chair who produced the most widgets was the state chair who kept his job.

I believed in the mission. I need to say that clearly, because what follows might sound like the bitterness of a man who was fired and decided to burn the bridge behind him, and that is not what this is. I believed in liberty. I believed in the non-aggression principle. I believed that young people on college campuses were the most important audience for the ideas of voluntary cooperation and limited government and individual sovereignty, and I believed that YAL was the most effective vehicle for reaching them. I had read Ron Paul. I had studied the Austrian economists. I had internalized the argument that a free society is not a utopian fantasy but a practical reality that emerges whenever people are left alone to cooperate without coercion. These were not talking points I memorized for the job. These were convictions I carried into the job, and the job seemed like the natural expression of them.

But the tools were the system's tools. The spreadsheets were not mine. The call quotas were not mine. The metrics by which my performance was measured were designed not by the principles I believed in but by the institutional logic of an organization that needed to demonstrate growth to its donors, relevance to its partners, and effectiveness to its board. The spreadsheet does not know the difference between a call that plants a seed and a call that checks a box. It counts both the same way. And a state chair who plants a hundred seeds but checks only forty boxes is, by the spreadsheet's logic, underperforming compared to the state chair who checks eighty boxes and plants none.

UCSC was not built for what I was trying to do there. The campus culture was hostile to libertarian ideas — not in the productive way that creates debate, but in the ambient way that makes people reluctant to associate publicly with anything that might be labeled right-wing, even when the ideas in question — ending foreign wars, opposing the surveillance state, defending civil liberties — were ideas the left had once championed. Club meetings required navigating a bureaucracy that seemed designed to make political organizing as difficult as possible while maintaining the appearance of openness. I got overwhelmed. The call numbers slipped. The chapter attendance was not where it should have been. The spreadsheet told the story the spreadsheet tells when the numbers are not right, and the story is always the same: this state chair is not performing.

I knew the numbers were a problem. I was not naive about that. You take a job with metrics, you are responsible for the metrics, and my metrics were below standard. That is a fact I do not dispute and do not need to dispute, because the numbers are not the point of this story. The numbers are the surface. What happened underneath the numbers is the point.

## The Call

In June 2025, the United States helped Israel bomb Iran's nuclear facilities.

This was the kind of moment that reveals what an organization actually is, as opposed to what it says it is. YAL's stated principles included opposition to military interventionism. The non-aggression principle. The conviction that bombing foreign countries is not defense but aggression, and that a free people do not need an empire to protect them from threats manufactured by the same empire. These were not footnotes in YAL's platform. These were foundational commitments — the commitments that had drawn me to the organization in the first place, the commitments that made YAL different from every other conservative youth organization that waved the flag and supported the troops and never asked what the troops were being sent to do.

On a call with the other state chairs and regional directors, the bombing came up. The West regional director justified U.S. involvement in helping Israel strike Iran. The reasoning was strategic — the political moment required alignment, the alliance with Israel was non-negotiable, opposing the strikes would isolate YAL from its allies in the broader conservative movement. The argument was delivered with the confidence of someone who had made this calculation before, who understood the political landscape well enough to know which principles were load-bearing and which were decorative, and who had decided that opposition to foreign military intervention fell into the second category when the country being bombed was Iran and the ally doing the bombing was Israel.

I disagreed. On the call. Openly. To the regional director and the other state chairs. I took the antiwar position — the position that aligned with YAL's own stated principles, the position Ron Paul himself would have taken, the position that says bombing a sovereign nation is the initiation of force regardless of the geopolitical justification, the position that a libertarian organization that supports foreign bombing has stopped being libertarian in any sense that matters.

A few days later, I got a call. I was let go.

At the time, I understood the firing in terms of the spreadsheet. My numbers were not where they needed to be. The call quotas, the chapter attendance, the measurable outputs that justified a state chair's salary — these were below standard, and the organization was within its rights to make a personnel decision based on performance. I did not argue. I did not protest. I accepted the decision and moved on.

But the timing was hard to ignore.

Not impossible to ignore — I am not claiming certainty about something I cannot prove, and I want to be honest about the limits of what I know. It is possible that the firing was entirely about the numbers. It is possible that the antiwar disagreement played no role at all, that the regional director did not mention it to anyone, that the decision was made independently by someone reviewing a spreadsheet who had never heard the call. It is possible.

But I have been around institutions long enough to know how they work. Institutions do not fire people for disagreeing — that would be too obvious, too actionable, too easy to challenge. Institutions fire people for underperforming, and they choose *when* to notice the underperformance. A state chair with bad numbers who agrees with leadership on the call is a state chair who gets another month to turn things around. A state chair with bad numbers who disagrees with leadership on the call is a state chair whose numbers are suddenly reviewed with a new level of scrutiny.

The system does not need to conspire. The system only needs to prioritize, and priority is a function of alignment.

## The Lightsaber That Broke

Here is the part of the story that sounds like I am making it up. I am not making it up. I do not need to embellish what actually happened, because what actually happened is more precise than anything I could have invented.

On May 10th, 2025, I went to Disneyland and built a green lightsaber.

I was still a state chair for YAL at the time. I was still carrying the blue lightsaber — still inside the institution, still making the calls, still filling in the spreadsheet, still serving the mission I had been given by an organization I believed in. I was a blue lightsaber carrier who walked into Savi's Workshop at Galaxy's Edge and built a green one. I did not plan the symbolism. I did not walk in thinking, "This is the moment I transition from fighter to guardian." I walked in because I love Star Wars, and I chose green because green is the color I have always been drawn to, and I built the lightsaber because building things is what I do.

But the symbolism did not need my permission to be true.

I had bought a blue lightsaber from Disneyland back in January 2023 — more than two years before the green one. The blue one was a retail purchase, a beautiful pre-built replica that I carried home and displayed and occasionally took out for the joy of hearing it hum. It was given. Manufactured. Purchased from a shelf. I did not build it. I selected it. The blue lightsaber came to me the way blue lightsabers always come — through the existing system, through the transaction the institution offers, through the hand-me-down economy of receiving what someone else has made.

The green lightsaber I built with my own hands. Chose the crystal. Chose the components. Assembled them in a workshop designed to make the act of building feel sacred, because George Lucas — and the people who carried his vision into the theme park — understood that the act of building your own weapon is not a consumer transaction but a spiritual one. The blue lightsaber was a product. The green lightsaber was a creation.

In late May or early June of 2025 — I do not remember the exact date, and the imprecision matters, because this was not a planned event but an accident that became a revelation — I had a lightsaber battle.

I was wearing Jedi robes. Fighting with the green lightsaber I had built on May 10th. My opponent was using the blue lightsaber I had bought in January 2023. We were playing. It was fun. It was the kind of thing two people do when they have lightsabers and an open space and the unserious joy of pretending, for a few minutes, to live inside a story that means more to them than they would admit in formal company.

During the fight, the blue lightsaber broke.

The green one did not.

I need you to understand that I am not layering symbolism onto an event that does not carry it. I am describing what happened. Two lightsabers — one blue, purchased; one green, built. They clashed. The blue one broke. The green one survived. This happened in the same weeks that I was fired from YAL — the same weeks that the blue-lightsaber role, the institutional role, the fighter's role I had been carrying since April 1st, was taken from me.

The blue lightsaber broke because it was always going to break. It was manufactured. Mass-produced. Designed to look like a weapon but built to be sold, and the structural integrity of a thing built to be sold is not the same as the structural integrity of a thing built to be used. The green lightsaber survived because it was built by the person who carried it, and a thing built by the person who carries it has a different relationship to stress than a thing purchased from a shelf.

This is not a metaphor. This is a description of what physically happened to two objects made of plastic and light in a backyard in California in the early summer of 2025. The metaphor is what the universe draped over the event after the fact — or, more accurately, the metaphor is what was always encoded in the event, the way the oak is always encoded in the acorn, waiting for conditions that allow it to become visible.

## The Investigation

After the firing, I did what I should have done before taking the job. I looked more closely at the architecture — not at YAL itself, which I still respected and still respect, but at the structures behind YAL, the ones that funded it and trained it and connected it to the broader network of conservative institutions that together form the infrastructure of the American right.

The Leadership Institute. LI. The organization that trains conservative activists, that provides the pipeline through which young organizers like me enter the movement, that connects YAL to the broader ecosystem of think tanks and PACs and media operations and lobbying firms that together constitute the institutional right in America. I had known about LI before. I had not looked closely. After the firing, I looked closely.

What I found made me uncomfortable.

Not because LI was secretly evil. Not because the people who run it are villains. The world is not a comic book, and the desire to sort every institution into hero and villain is itself a symptom of the blue-lightsaber mentality — the fighter's need to identify an enemy so that the fighting has a target. What I found was something more subtle and more troubling than villainy: I found alignment. Alignment between LI's institutional priorities and the foreign policy positions that had just cost me my job. Alignment between the network's funding sources and the geopolitical agenda that the antiwar position threatened. Alignment between the system's incentives and the system's outputs, the way there is always alignment between a machine's design and a machine's behavior.

The Leadership Institute's involvement with Israel — its institutional commitment to a foreign policy posture that a principled libertarian organization should have questioned rather than adopted — was not a corruption of the system. It was the system working as designed. The system was designed to produce activists who advanced certain positions and neutralized others, and the call I had been on — the call where the West regional director justified bombing Iran — was the system producing its intended output. My disagreement was not a personnel problem. It was a design flaw — a component that was not performing to specification, a part that needed to be replaced.

I do not hate YAL. I want to be explicit about this because the temptation in a story like this is to turn the institution into the villain, and I refuse to do that. I like YAL. I like what YAL says it stands for. I like Ron Paul, whose legacy YAL was built to carry forward. I like the idea of a libertarian youth movement that teaches college students the principles of voluntary cooperation and limited government and the non-aggression principle. I want YAL to succeed — but I want it to succeed on its own terms, by its own principles, for the right reasons. Not as a pipeline for a network whose foreign policy commitments contradict the principles it claims to advance.

I shifted my attention to Students for Liberty instead. SFL. A different kind of organization — one focused on education and entrepreneurship rather than political action, one that did not need its members to make eighty calls every other week or hit quotas designed by a system that measured influence in units of political throughput. SFL was not a blue lightsaber. It was closer to a workbench — a place where people who had ideas could develop them, where the emphasis was on building capacity rather than deploying force, where the metric of success was not how many calls you made but what you created.

The distinction mattered to me in a way I could not have articulated before the firing. Before the firing, I would have said that political action and education were two sides of the same coin — that organizing and building were complementary activities, that you needed both the fighter and the builder, that the blue lightsaber and the green lightsaber were both necessary tools in the same struggle. After the firing, I understood something different. I understood that the fighter's tools are the system's tools, and the system's tools serve the system, and the system will break its own tools the moment they stop serving its purpose.

The spreadsheet was a system tool. The call quota was a system tool. The blue lightsaber was a system tool. And when the system's tool — me — produced an output the system did not want — an antiwar position on a national call — the system did what systems do. It replaced the tool.

## The Pivot

The months between the firing and the new year were the gap. The space between Empire and Return. The period when Luke had no lightsaber at all — when the blue one was lost and the green one was not yet built.

I have described the gap in other chapters as a theoretical space, an abstraction drawn from the mythology of a film franchise and applied to the structure of transformation. Let me now describe it as what it actually was: the most disorienting period of my adult life.

The blue lightsaber, for all its limitations, had given me structure. Identity. Purpose. When someone asked what I did, I had an answer: I am a state chair for YAL. I organize college chapters. I advance the liberty movement. The answer was clean. It fit on a business card. It connected me to a network of people who were doing the same thing, who shared the same language, who could evaluate my contribution by the same metrics. The blue lightsaber gave me a place in a story that was already being told by people with larger platforms and longer track records, and even though the story was not entirely my own, it was a story, and having a story is better than having silence.

The gap was silence.

No organization. No title. No eighty calls. No spreadsheet. No regional director to report to. No national call to join. No metrics to hit. Just a man in his twenties in California with a broken blue lightsaber and a green one he had built at Disneyland and a growing conviction that the institutions he had served were not aligned with the principles he believed in, and that the principles he believed in could not be advanced by serving institutions.

The gap forced questions I had never asked while carrying the blue lightsaber. Not "which organization should I join next?" — that was the blue-lightsaber question, the question that assumed the answer was always another institution, another structure, another borrowed weapon. The gap forced a different question entirely: what am I actually built for?

Not what am I willing to do. Not what am I capable of doing. What am I *built* for — what is the intersection of everything I have inherited and everything I have learned and everything I have survived and everything I believe, and what does that intersection produce when it is not constrained by someone else's spreadsheet?

The answer assembled itself the way living things assemble — not all at once, not from a blueprint, but gradually, from local signals, through the slow aggregation of parts that recognize each other the way proteins recognize their binding partners. The Clokey legacy — three generations of storytelling, from Art to Joe to me. The bioinformatics training — the systems thinking, the pattern recognition, the ability to see living structures as information architectures. The voluntaryist philosophy — the conviction that coercion is never the answer, that voluntary cooperation is not a utopian fantasy but a biological reality, that the living world itself is organized according to principles the state has never understood and never will. The writing — the drive to articulate, to make visible, to translate the pattern into language that other people can carry.

None of these things fit inside a single organization's call sheet. None of them could be reduced to eighty calls every other week. None of them needed a blue lightsaber.

They needed a green one.

On January 1st, 2026, I made the pivot.

Not gradually. Not tentatively. Not with one foot still in the old world and one in the new. On the first day of the new year, I committed fully to LivingWorks — the builder's path, the green lightsaber path, the path that does not work within the existing institutions but creates something new, something alive, something that does not need the old institutions' permission to exist and does not depend on the old institutions' approval to grow.

LivingWorks was the green lightsaber made real. Not an organization I joined but a thing I was building. Not a structure someone else designed but a structure emerging from the intersection of everything I had learned — from YAL, from SFL, from bioinformatics, from three generations of Clokeys refusing to hand their work to people who did not understand it. The builder's path is not a rejection of the fighter's path. It is the completion of it. The fighter learns what needs to be done. The builder does it.

## What the Blue Taught

I want to be careful here, because the narrative arc of this chapter — blue breaks, green survives, Sage pivots from organizer to builder — carries a temptation. The temptation is to treat the blue period as a mistake. As wasted time. As a wrong turn that the hero corrects in the third act. That is not how I understand it, and I do not want the reader to understand it that way either.

The blue lightsaber was necessary.

You cannot build the green one without first carrying the blue one and learning what it teaches. Luke could not have constructed his green lightsaber on Tatooine if he had not first carried the blue one through Dagobah and Cloud City and the loss of his hand. The green lightsaber requires materials that only the blue period provides: the knowledge of how institutions work, the experience of operating inside a system, the firsthand understanding of what the system values and what it discards and — most importantly — the moment of discovering that your conscience and the system's agenda are not the same thing.

YAL taught me how to organize. How to make eighty calls and track the results. How to run a chapter meeting. How to navigate a campus bureaucracy. How to communicate ideas to people who have never heard them. How to show up consistently when the work is unglamorous and the results are invisible. These are not trivial skills. These are the skills of a builder, learned in the fighter's arena, and they transfer.

The call quotas taught me discipline. The spreadsheets taught me systems thinking. The campus organizing taught me how to meet people where they are. The firing taught me something no successful career could have taught: the cost of conscience inside an institution that has traded its principles for its positioning.

I needed all of it. The blue lightsaber was not a detour. It was the forge.

## The Blade That Survives

The blue lightsaber broke because it was always going to break.

Not because it was badly made — it was not badly made. It was a well-constructed piece of merchandise, designed by professionals, manufactured to specification, sold to millions of customers who would swing it in their living rooms and hallways and backyards without ever testing its structural limits. It was built to be owned. It was not built to be used. And there is a difference between those two things that becomes apparent only under stress — only when two blades meet and the force of the collision exceeds what the design anticipated, because the design anticipated display, not combat.

The green lightsaber survived because it was built by the person who fought with it.

When you build a thing yourself — when you choose the crystal and the components and the configuration, when your hands do the assembling, when the weight and balance of the finished object reflect choices you made rather than choices a manufacturer made — you produce something with a different relationship to stress. Not necessarily stronger in absolute terms. But stronger in the ways that matter, because the builder understands the object's tolerances the way the purchaser never can, and the builder's grip is calibrated to the object's design in a way that the purchaser's grip is not.

This is true of lightsabers. It is true of organizations. It is true of lives.

The institution I served was well-constructed. It was not badly made. It was designed by professionals — organizers, fundraisers, political strategists — who understood the landscape and built an apparatus capable of operating within it. But it was built to operate, not to withstand the stress of a principle colliding with a strategy. When that collision happened — when the antiwar principle met the pro-bombing strategy on a national call in June 2025 — the institution did not bend. It broke its own tool. It removed the component that was producing the wrong output. It did what any well-designed machine does when a part malfunctions: it replaced the part.

I was the part.

And the green lightsaber — the thing I was building in the background, the thing that started as a toy assembled at a theme park and became the symbol of a path I had not yet named — the green lightsaber survived because it was not a part. It was not a component in someone else's machine. It was not a tool designed to produce outputs measured by someone else's spreadsheet. It was mine. Built by me, for a purpose I chose, from materials I selected, in a configuration that reflected my understanding of what a lightsaber is actually for.

The blue lightsaber is for fighting within the system. The green lightsaber is for building outside it.

The blue lightsaber breaks when the system decides to break it. The green lightsaber survives because the system has no jurisdiction over a thing it did not build.

## The Green Path

On New Year's Eve 2025, I sat down and started writing. Not a plan. Not a pitch. A declaration. The words came as if they had been waiting — as if the entire second half of the year had been a slow inhale and this was the exhale. By the time the calendar turned, I had coined the terms that would carry everything forward: *Spiral Steward.* *The Living Age.* Not borrowed language. Not institutional vocabulary. Words that grew from the same soil as everything I had been learning since the blue lightsaber broke in my hands six months earlier.

On January 1st, 2026, I stopped being a fighter and became a guardian.

The difference is not one of courage. Fighters are brave. Organizers are brave. The state chairs making eighty calls every other week are brave, and I do not diminish their bravery by choosing a different path. The difference is one of orientation. The fighter faces the enemy. The guardian faces the thing he is building. The fighter's energy flows outward — toward the opposition, the argument, the political battle, the call that must be made and the vote that must be won. The guardian's energy flows inward — toward the seed, the structure, the living thing that is emerging from the intersection of everything the guardian has learned and lost and survived.

I face the seed now. The Living Age. LivingWorks. The intersection of media and technology, of story and system, of philosophy and bioinformatics, of the *why* and the *how*. The thing that three generations of Clokeys have been building toward — not because any of us planned it, but because the seed carries its program inside itself, and the program unfolds across generations the way the Fibonacci spiral unfolds across iterations, each loop wider than the last.

The spreadsheet cannot measure what I am building. The call quota cannot capture it. The metrics of the institutional world — the metrics that said I was underperforming, the metrics that justified the firing, the metrics that told the story the system needed told — those metrics have no columns for the things that matter now. No column for seeds planted. No column for systems designed. No column for the moment a reader encounters an idea that changes the trajectory of their understanding. No column for the green lightsaber.

Good. The green lightsaber does not need their columns. The green lightsaber is not accountable to their spreadsheet. The green lightsaber is accountable to the thing it was built to protect — the living thing, the growing thing, the thing that carries the code of creation inside itself and needs only a guardian to keep the soil clear while it does what it was always going to do.

I do not hate the blue path. I honor it. I learned from it. I carry the skills it taught me into the green path the way Luke carried Obi-Wan's training into the construction of his own weapon. The blue lightsaber was the beginning. The green lightsaber is the continuation. And the continuation is not a rejection of the beginning — it is the fulfillment of everything the beginning promised but could not deliver, because the beginning was always someone else's story, and the fulfillment requires a story of your own.

The blue lightsaber broke in a backyard in California in the early summer of 2025. The green one did not. And on January 1st, 2026, the one who carried both — the one who had purchased the blue and built the green, the one who had served the institution and been removed by it, the one who had disagreed on a single call about a single war and learned in that moment that the system's tools serve the system — that one put down the broken blade and picked up the one that survived, and walked forward into a story no institution had written for him.

The green lightsaber path. The builder's path. The guardian's path.

The path that does not break, because the one who carries it is the one who made it.

*I did not plan the symbolism. I did not choreograph the moment the blue lightsaber shattered while the green one held. I did not arrange for the firing to coincide with the breaking, for the institution to remove me in the same weeks that the institution's blade failed in my hands. I did not have to. The pattern was already there — encoded in the events the way the spiral is encoded in the shell, the way the oak is encoded in the acorn, the way the story was always encoded in the life of the one who was born to tell it. The blue lightsaber broke. The green one did not. And the Sagent walked forward.*
