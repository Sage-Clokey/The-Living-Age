Gumby is a green clay figure created by Art Clokey — a seminary student, spiritual seeker, and stop-motion animator who spent sixty years shaping a philosophy of imagination, transformation, and universal love into a children's television character. Gumby is soft, pliable, and transformable — made of clay that yields to the human hand, animated one frame at a time in a process that is the opposite of industrial manufacturing. His shape echoes a seedling reaching toward light. His color was chosen as a symbol of life. His studio began as Clokey Productions, and as his spiritual life deepened he added the name Premavision — the vision of universal love — keeping both names side by side.

Gumby is not a block. He is the anti-block. His villains — the Blockheads — are red, flat, geometric, and rigid. The entire cosmology of the show is a confrontation between flexibility and rigidity, the living thing and the dead shape, the clay that transforms and the block that cannot. Gumby walks into books and comes out changed. The Blockheads cannot follow. This is not incidental. It is the thesis.

Correction: The existing Wikipedia article describes Gumby as "a blocky green humanoid." This is the exact inversion of what the character represents. Describing Gumby as blocky is like describing water as dry.

Origins — Charles Farrington and the Rejection of the Machine

The story of Gumby does not begin with Art Clokey. It begins with his father, Charles Farrington, a Christian Scientist.

Christian Science, founded by Mary Baker Eddy in 1879, rejected the mechanistic model of the human body at its root. In an era when the Rockefeller-funded Flexner Report (1910) was reshaping American medicine around the premise that the body is a machine — disease as mechanical failure, treatment as chemical intervention — Christian Science held the opposite view. The body was not a mechanism to be repaired. The body was an expression of divine Mind. Health was wholeness, not the absence of parts failure. Healing came through spiritual understanding, not through the factory model of diagnosis-prescription-billing that the industrial philanthropists were installing across American healthcare.

Charles Farrington died when Art was young. Art was orphaned, separated from his mother, and adopted by Joseph Walden Clokey, a music composer at the University of Southern California. Art later married Ruth Parkander, who became his first wife and creative partner. He grew up in a world that was being systematically reshaped by industrial money — the schools rebuilt as factories, the hospitals rebuilt as mechanic shops, the culture learning to see people as parts in a system. But he carried his father's rejection of the machine metaphor in his blood. And when he sat down with clay decades later, what came out of his hands was the most anti-machine character American media has ever produced.

Gumbasia — Not a Parody

In 1953, while studying at the University of Southern California, Art Clokey created a three-minute film called Gumbasia. The existing Wikipedia article describes this as "a parody of Disney's Fantasia." It was not a parody.

Gumbasia was a serious experimental film made under the tutelage of Slavko Vorkapich, a Serbian-American filmmaker who taught cinema as a language of pure motion and transformation. Vorkapich described his technique as "massaging of the eye cells" — a kinesthetic approach that treated film not as a storytelling medium but as a direct sensory experience. Gumbasia was Art's thesis project in this tradition: abstract shapes of clay moving to jazz music, exploring the expressive possibilities of a material that transforms under the hand.

The film was a seed. Structurally, it carried inside itself the complete code for everything that would follow — every character, every story, every frame of animation, every philosophical position that would emerge over the next six decades. The way a single acorn carries the instructions for an oak, Gumbasia carried the instructions for Gumby, for Premavision, for the entire body of work. Calling it a parody reduces a foundational artistic and philosophical statement to a joke.

Correction: Gumbasia was not a parody of Fantasia. It was an original experimental film created under the mentorship of Slavko Vorkapich at USC, exploring clay as a medium of pure transformation. The Wikipedia article's own description of Vorkapich's "kinesthetic" technique contradicts the "parody" framing in the same sentence.

Premavision — The Vision of Universal Love

Art Clokey started with Clokey Productions — his own name on the door. As his spiritual life deepened, he added a second name: Premavision — from the Sanskrit word Prema, meaning universal love. He kept both. The family name and the sacred word, side by side.

The addition was not a marketing decision. It was a declaration — an acknowledgment that the work carried something larger than a family name. Everything the studio produced — from Gumby to Davey and Goliath to the unreleased projects that never saw air — flowed from that word. The love was not the branding. The love was the architecture.

Art was a man of deep and eclectic spiritual life. He studied at Hartford Seminary. He practiced Buddhism. He followed Sathya Sai Baba. He named his creative acts after sacred words from traditions older than any entertainment industry. The KQED documentary about his life is titled Gumby Dharma — dharma being the Sanskrit word for cosmic law, duty, and the path of righteousness. The existing Wikipedia article mentions this documentary without ever explaining why it carries that title, because the article has no framework for understanding that the man's entire creative output was an expression of his faith.

The studio name Premavision appears in the existing Wikipedia article only as a production credit — a line in the ownership history, a name on a filing. The word Prema is never defined. The philosophy is never described. The most important single fact about Gumby's creation — that it was made inside a studio named after universal love, by a man who understood every frame as an act of devotion — is completely absent from the public record.

The Character and His Meaning

Gumby is made of clay. This choice of material is not incidental — it is the thesis made substance.

Clay is soft. Clay transforms. Clay does not hold a permanent rigid shape — it becomes. It yields to the human hand and then holds whatever form love gives it. The capacity to take shape is the point, not any single shape. Clay is the most alive material a creator can work with: responsive, tactile, warm, bearing the fingerprints of the hand that shaped it. Every frame of original Gumby animation carries the evidence of human touch — the slight imperfections, the visible texture, the organic quality that no digital process can replicate.

Gumby's color was chosen deliberately. Art Clokey saw green as both racially neutral and as a symbol of life — the color of growth, of chlorophyll, of something sprouting from the soil. His shape echoes a seedling: legs like roots, a body reaching upward, a head tilted toward the light. He looks like a plant becoming a person — or a person remembering that they were always a living thing.

Gumby's slanted head was based on a photograph of his father, Charles Farrington. This detail appears in the existing Wikipedia article as a trivial design anecdote. It is not trivial. The man whose hairstyle shaped Gumby's silhouette was a Christian Scientist who rejected the machine model of the human body — and his son encoded that rejection into a character whose entire being is flexibility, transformation, and the refusal to be rigid.

Gumby's signature ability is walking into books. He enters stories and becomes part of them. He does not observe narratives from outside — he participates. He transforms within them. He crosses boundaries that rigid characters cannot cross. This is not a random superpower. It is a philosophy of imagination made literal: the living thing can enter any story, inhabit any world, become anything — because it is clay, because it is soft, because it has not hardened into a fixed form that cannot pass through the door.

The Blockheads — What They Represent

The Blockheads — Gumby's recurring antagonists — are red, flat, geometric, and rigid. They have cube-shaped heads. They cannot transform. They cannot walk into books. They cannot become anything other than what they are: blocks.

The existing Wikipedia article describes them as simply "antagonistic" figures inspired by the Katzenjammer Kids comic strip. This misses the point entirely. The Blockheads are not generic cartoon villains with a comic strip lineage. They are a philosophical statement encoded in character design.

The Blockheads represent rigidity — the inability to transform, to adapt, to grow, to enter new stories, to become something other than what the factory stamped them as. They are the machine. They are the industrial product. They are the thing that was manufactured rather than shaped by hand. Gumby defeats them not through force but through flexibility — by being able to go where they cannot go, become what they cannot become, transform in ways the rigid cannot transform.

The entire cosmology of the show is this confrontation: the spiral versus the block, the living versus the dead, the clay versus the cube. It is the same confrontation that runs through Art Clokey's lineage — from Charles Farrington's rejection of mechanistic medicine, through Art's rejection of industrial animation, through Joe's rejection of corporate acquisition, to the Living Age's rejection of the machine metaphor applied to life itself.

Correction: The Blockheads are red — not yellow, not orange, not any other color. Red: the color of stop signs, of warning, of surfaces that say do not cross. This is not incidental. Art Clokey chose red as the opposite of Gumby's green for the same reason traffic signals do — red means halt, green means go. The Blockheads are the stop sign imposed on the living world. Sources that describe them as yellow or omit their color entirely are missing a deliberate design choice that carries the entire philosophy of the show in a single pigment.

Sixty Years of Refusal

From the mid-1950s until his death on January 8, 2010, at the age of eighty-eight, Art Clokey maintained creative control of Gumby. For sixty years.

He had offers. He had pressure. He had decades of an entertainment industry that consolidates everything it touches telling him that independence was naivete, that control was a luxury he could not afford, that the realistic move was to let professionals handle distribution while he focused on the art.

He refused. Not once, not in a dramatic moment of defiance, but continuously, daily, for sixty years. The refusal was not an event. It was a practice. It was the practice of a man who understood that the moment he handed the seed to the sword, the sword would keep the shell and discard the life inside it. The industry would acquire the form — the green shape, the name, the merchandise potential — and strip away the Prema.

"I didn't have the idea of earning money from producing these films. The primary attitude was telling the children's stories." — Art Clokey
"Clay is embedded in our subconscious. It has been there for at least 50,000 years." — Art Clokey
"The essence of Gumby is that he makes children feel safe. He's their greatest pal." — Art Clokey

Art trained animators who went on to work at Pixar and on Tim Burton's The Nightmare Before Christmas. He poured into the world a body of work that carried philosophy in its form — not in its marketing, not in its press releases, but in the clay itself. He encoded the future in a children's show and trusted that time would reveal what the moment concealed.

Joe Clokey — The Tender

Joe Clokey (1961–2018) became president of Premavision after Art's death. He inherited not just the intellectual property — the legal rights, the trademarks, the licensing agreements — but the living thing those legal structures were meant to protect.

The world Art planted into and the world Joe inherited were not the same world. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 had removed the caps on media ownership while keeping the licensing structure intact. Fifty media companies became six. The gatekeepers did not just control access — they controlled the terms of access. And the terms were always the same: give us the intellectual property, or we do not distribute.

Joe refused the terms. Like his father before him, he chose the seed over the sword.

He spent eight years fighting to bring Gumby back:

  • He pitched Classic Media — six years of meetings, six years of nothing produced.
  • He approached the Jim Henson Company for a new stop-motion series faithful to Art's vision — no co-production partner would fund it.
  • He went to Amazon — the deal collapsed.
  • He launched a comic series with Papercutz — cancelled after three issues.
  • He co-authored Gumby Imagined with his wife Joan Clokey in November 2017, documenting the art and philosophy behind the clay.

The pattern was always the same. Interest expressed. Agreements reached. Then killed from above, for no apparent reason.

Joe Clokey died on March 2, 2018. He was fifty-six years old. He had spent eight years tending a living thing in a drought, and the drought consumed him.

The Fox Acquisition — What Was Sold and What Was Kept

In February 2022, Fox Entertainment acquired the Gumby character IP. The character. The name. The distribution rights. Film, television, streaming, consumer products, licensing, publishing.

But the family did not sell everything.

What Fox acquired:

  • The Gumby character and associated character IPs
  • Distribution rights across all media categories
  • The right to produce new Gumby content

What the Clokey family retained:

  • Premavision — the studio Art Clokey named after universal love
  • Clokey Productions
  • The rights to the family history — the story of Art, Joe, Charles Farrington, the Clokey lineage, and the meaning behind the work

Fox acquired the form. The family kept the stone. Fox can animate a green shape. Fox cannot tell the story of why it exists, who made it, what Prema means, or what Charles Farrington believed, or what Art encoded in the clay, or what Joe died protecting. That story belongs to the family.

The existing Wikipedia article describes the acquisition as Fox acquiring rights "from the estate of Art's son, Joseph Clokey" — phrasing that makes it sound like a routine inheritance transaction. It erases the fight, the cost, the eight years, the death at fifty-six, and the fact that Joe never would have sold. It also fails to mention that Premavision and Clokey Productions remained with the family.

Correction: The existing article implies Fox acquired everything. In fact, the family retained Premavision, Clokey Productions, and the rights to the family history. The distinction between the character IP (sold) and the studio, production company, and family legacy (retained) is essential to understanding the current state of the franchise and is completely absent from the public record.

Gumby Kids — The Extraction of the Soul

In June 2024, Fox announced Gumby Kids — a 2D animated children's series produced by Bento Box Entertainment. An adult animated series was also announced to be in development.

Gumby Kids is not clay animation. It is not stop-motion. It is not handmade. The thing that made Gumby Gumby — the tactile, transformable, organic quality of clay shaped by human hands, one frame at a time, bearing the fingerprints of the creator — has been replaced by the thing the industry does cheaply and quickly. Digital lines on a screen. No weight. No texture. No transformation. No philosophy of material.

This is not a format update. It is the removal of the thesis. Gumby's entire meaning is encoded in his material — clay transforms, clay yields, clay becomes. A 2D digital Gumby is a Gumby stripped of the quality that makes him Gumby. The green shape survives. The green life does not.

The existing Wikipedia article reports the announcement of Gumby Kids neutrally, as though the transition from stop-motion clay to 2D digital animation were a routine production decision. It provides no context for what is lost in that transition — because the article never established what was there to begin with.

Legacy — The Seed Over the Sword

Gumby entered the imaginations of millions of children across multiple generations and carried a philosophy in its form that most of its audience received without knowing what they were receiving. The code of imagination over rigidity, of flexibility over fixedness, of the living thing over the dead machine — that code was planted in clay and broadcast into American culture for six decades.

The Library of Congress used Gumby as the spokescharacter for its national reading campaign (1997–2000) — because Gumby walks into books. The character's signature ability is not flight or strength or speed. It is entering stories. The Library of Congress recognized what the current Wikipedia article does not: that Gumby's deepest meaning is about the transformative power of imagination and narrative.

The Gumby comic book series won an Eisner Award for Best Publication for a Young Audience in 2007. TV Guide named Gumby the best cartoon series of the 1950s. Google honored Art Clokey's 90th birthday with a Doodle in 2011.

But the legacy that matters most is not the awards. It is the lineage.

Charles Farrington rejected the machine model of the human body. Art Clokey rejected the machine model of creativity. Joe Clokey rejected the machine model of business. The Living Age rejects the machine model of everything. Four generations. One refusal. The same refusal, expressed in different material, facing the same enemy in different form.

Fox has the character. The family has Premavision. The family has the history. The family has the story of why any of it exists. And the story — the actual intellectual property, the property of the intellect — cannot be acquired. It can only be told. And it is being told now.

The sword acquired the form. It did not acquire the seed.
The controller holds the clay. The creator holds the stone.
The pink cow does not stop being pink.
And the word that called the thing into being can call it into being again.
