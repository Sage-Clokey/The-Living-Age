Subject: The Living Age — connecting Gumby, BMEB, and what comes next

Dr. Bernick,

I wanted to share something with you that connects what we've been talking about in the book club to what I've been building and where I want to take it.

You told me that you originally designed BMEB to integrate biology and computation as one program — and that other faculty took over and separated the two sides. That stuck with me, because the same pattern runs through my family's story and through the project I've been working on.

My grandfather Art Clokey created Gumby. My father spent years trying to continue it after Art passed, and he was stopped at every turn until he died in 2018. Gumby was sold to Fox Entertainment in 2022. But the part that matters for this conversation isn't the business side — it's why my father refused to ever compromise on Gumby being clay, formed by real hands.

Gumby is Genesis. Formed from the dirt, shaped by hand, given life through touch. That wasn't an animation preference — it was the meaning embedded in the medium. Clay is earth. The creator forms it. It becomes something alive. You can't digitize that and keep what it is.

I've been sitting with that for a long time, and it led me somewhere I didn't expect — into biology itself. If clay was the metaphor, DNA is the reality. Life is literally formed from the earth, organized not by a central controller but through distributed, self-organizing order. Genes are expressed, not executed. DNA is read, not processed. The whole system is linguistic, not mechanical.

This is where Austrian economics comes in — and why the book club has mattered more to me than you probably realize. Hayek's insight about spontaneous order is a description of how living systems actually work. No central planner coordinates the economy. No master gene coordinates the organism. In both cases, order emerges from local actors responding to local signals. The morphogen gradient is a price system. The gene regulatory network is a market. The knowledge problem applies to cells the same way it applies to firms.

I've been attempting to build a genome design system called the Adaptive Genome Design System. It retrieves real genomic data from UCSC and NCBI, scores compatibility across species, and designs assemblies for synthetic constructs. The computational pipeline works in theory, but it's not grounded in reality yet — it hasn't been validated at the bench. I know what I'm missing. I can design on a screen, but I've never closed the loop with actual wet lab work.

I've been thinking about whether a PhD in BMEB — the program you built to be integrated — could be the right place to ground this. Would there be a way to bridge computational genome design with wet lab validation through the program? The philosophy is written, but what I'm missing is the hands-on biology and the institutional resources to take this from in silico to in vivo.

You built BMEB to keep biology and computation together. My father kept Gumby as clay because the medium carries the meaning. I'm trying to build something that holds both sides together — the science and the philosophy, the computation and the living system — and doesn't let them be separated.

I want to turn this into a real company — and I've been thinking about whether a PhD program could serve as an incubator of sorts, where the research and the business grow together. What would you say is the best way to go about something like that?

I'd love to hear your advice. I'll see you at book club Monday.

Sage Clokey
